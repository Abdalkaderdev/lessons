# Using AI Responsibly
### Power comes with a responsibility to think before you click

You now know how to use AI effectively. This lesson covers the other side: how to use it *ethically* — protecting yourself, being fair to others, and building real skills along the way.

None of this is about scaring you away from AI. It's about using it in a way you can be proud of.

---

## Part 1 — Your Privacy and AI

### What Happens When You Type Something Into AI?

When you type a message into [claude.ai](https://claude.ai), [chatgpt.com](https://chatgpt.com), or [gemini.google.com](https://gemini.google.com), that text is sent to a company's servers. It may be:

- Stored and reviewed by humans for safety monitoring
- Used to improve the model (depending on your settings)
- Retained for a period of time by the company

This isn't hidden — it's in the terms of service. But most people don't read those.

**The rule is simple: don't share information with AI that you wouldn't be comfortable with a stranger reading.**

### What NOT to Share with AI

- [ ] Your full name + address + phone number in the same message
- [ ] Passwords, PINs, or security questions
- [ ] Your social security number or government ID numbers
- [ ] Bank account or credit card details
- [ ] Medical information that could identify you (especially combined with your name)
- [ ] Private information about other people without their consent
- [ ] Confidential work or school documents that belong to an organization
- [ ] Photos that contain visible personal information (addresses, IDs)

### How to Protect Yourself While Still Getting Help

You can almost always get what you need without oversharing.

**Instead of:** "My name is Sarah Johnson, I live at 123 Main St, and I'm trying to write a complaint letter about my landlord Mark Davis at Davis Properties..."

**Write:** "I'm writing a formal complaint letter to my landlord about repeated failure to fix a heating system. I've sent written requests twice with no response. What should I include and what tone should I use?"

Same result. Zero personal exposure.

## Part 2 — Deepfakes and Misinformation

### What Is a Deepfake?

A **deepfake** is AI-generated media — a video, audio clip, or image — that makes it look or sound like a real person said or did something they never actually said or did.

The technology has become accessible enough that anyone with a laptop and free time can create convincing deepfakes. You don't need to be a programmer.

**Real harms deepfakes cause:**
- Non-consensual intimate images (putting someone's face on content they didn't consent to — this is illegal in many places)
- Political disinformation (fake videos of politicians saying things they never said)
- Scams using a person's voice to deceive family members ("grandparent scam" — a fake call from a "grandchild" in trouble)
- Reputation damage to private individuals
- Fake "evidence" in legal or personal disputes

### How to Spot a Deepfake

No technique is foolproof, but these signals help:

**Visual tells:**
- Unnatural blinking (too much or too little)
- Edges of the face or hair look slightly blurry or "floaty"
- Lighting on the face doesn't match the background
- Teeth look oddly uniform or strange
- Expressions don't quite sync with the words

**Context tells:**
- The video is shared without an original source
- The "original" can't be found anywhere credible
- The behavior shown is dramatically out of character
- It only appears on partisan or unknown websites

**Tools to verify:**
- Reverse image search (right-click > "Search image" in Chrome)
- [Google's Fact Check Tools](https://toolbox.google.com/factcheck/explorer)
- Snopes ([snopes.com](https://www.snopes.com)) for viral claims
- InVID/WeVerify browser extension for video verification

### The Broader Misinformation Problem

Deepfakes are just one form of AI-assisted misinformation. AI can also:
- Generate fake news articles at scale
- Write convincing social media posts from fake accounts
- Create fake reviews
- Generate fake academic papers

**The most important protection is critical thinking, not any single tool.** Ask:
- Where did this originate?
- Who benefits from people believing this?
- Can I find it confirmed by a credible primary source?
- Does this confirm a belief I already hold a little too perfectly?

That last question matters. We are all more likely to believe things that confirm what we already think. Bad actors know this and design misinformation accordingly.

## Part 3 — AI Bias

### What Is AI Bias?

AI systems are trained on human-created data. Human-created data contains centuries of human bias — against certain genders, races, nationalities, ages, body types, economic backgrounds, and more.

When AI learns from biased data, it learns the biases too. And because it operates at scale — potentially affecting millions of people — those biases get amplified.

### Real Examples of AI Bias

**Hiring:** Amazon built an AI recruiting tool that learned from 10 years of hiring data. Most of that data was from a male-dominated tech industry. The tool learned to downrank resumes that included the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club") and penalized graduates of all-women's colleges. Amazon scrapped the tool in 2018.

**Healthcare:** An algorithm used by US hospitals was found to be less likely to recommend additional care for Black patients compared to white patients with the same level of illness. The algorithm used cost as a proxy for need — and historical data showed less had been spent on Black patients, partly due to systemic inequality.

**Image generation:** Early image generators, when asked to depict "a CEO" or "a lawyer," produced predominantly white male images. When asked for "a criminal" or "a janitor," the racial and class disparities were stark.

**Facial recognition:** Studies by the MIT Media Lab found that some commercial facial recognition systems had error rates of up to 34% for darker-skinned women, compared to under 1% for lighter-skinned men. In real-world deployment, this has led to false arrests.

### Why This Matters for You

- If you use AI to help evaluate people, resumes, or work — check whether the criteria are actually fair
- If AI-generated images or text consistently represent certain groups in certain ways, that shapes perception
- Outputs from AI tools in hiring, criminal justice, healthcare, and education can affect people's lives
- You have the right to ask: "How was this AI system tested for bias?"

### What You Can Do

> Prompt: "Does your response reflect any assumptions about the demographic background of the person I'm describing? If so, identify them and give me a more balanced version."

> Prompt: "If I asked you this same question but specified the person was [different gender / ethnicity / age], would your answer change? Why or why not?"

Challenge AI outputs. Notice patterns. Speak up when something seems off.

## Part 4 — Academic Integrity

### The Honest Conversation

Let's be direct: every school and university is grappling with AI right now. Policies are being written and rewritten. What's allowed varies by school, by teacher, and by assignment.

**This lesson isn't going to tell you what your specific rules are — that's between you and your school. But here's a framework for thinking it through:**

### The Difference Between Using AI as a Tool vs. Cheating

**Using AI as a tool (generally acceptable):**
- Using AI to understand a concept you're confused about
- Asking AI to check your grammar and punctuation
- Using AI to brainstorm ideas before you write
- Asking AI to explain why your argument might be weak
- Using AI to research a topic (then verifying and synthesizing yourself)
- Using AI to format or organize notes you took

**Using AI in ways that compromise your education (often considered cheating):**
- Submitting AI-generated text as your own writing
- Having AI complete assessments meant to test your understanding
- Using AI during a test or exam when that's prohibited
- Passing off AI-generated work as evidence of a skill you haven't developed

### Why This Matters Beyond the Rules

Here's the part people skip: AI shortcuts can rob you of your own development.

Writing an essay is not just about producing text. It's about learning to construct an argument, develop a voice, handle complexity, and think under pressure. If you outsource that to AI, you skip the growth.

A student who has AI write all their essays ends up in a job interview with no writing samples they can genuinely explain, and no writing skill to back up their resume.

**The goal is to develop your own capability alongside AI — so you can do things AI can't, direct AI better than others can, and catch AI when it's wrong.**

> Prompt to use ethically: "I wrote this essay myself. Can you identify the three weakest arguments and ask me questions that help me strengthen them — without rewriting anything for me?"

This uses AI as a coach, not a ghostwriter.

## Part 5 — Copyright and AI-Generated Content

### Who Owns AI Outputs?

This is genuinely unsettled legal territory. Here's what we know right now (as of 2026):

**You generally can't copyright pure AI output** — in most jurisdictions, copyright requires human authorship. A fully AI-generated image or text has no clear copyright owner.

**You can copyright work where AI was a tool** — if you used AI as part of a creative process where you made substantial creative choices, the human-authored portions may be protectable.

**AI was trained on copyrighted material** — this is actively being litigated. Several lawsuits are ongoing from artists, authors, and news organizations who argue AI companies used their copyrighted work without permission.

### Practical Implications

**For using AI-generated images in your work:**
- Check the terms of the platform you generated them on
- Some platforms (like Adobe Firefly) were built with licensed training data and offer commercial-use rights
- For commercial projects, document how you created the image

**For AI-generated text:**
- If you publish or sell it, be aware that copyright protection may be limited
- Don't represent AI-generated writing as your own if you're selling it as "your" creative work to someone who expects original human creation
- Academic and journalistic standards generally require disclosure

**For training data:**
- If you're creating AI tools, use datasets with appropriate licenses
- The "scrape everything" approach is increasingly legally risky

## Part 6 — Building Your Own Skills Alongside AI

### The Risk of AI Dependency

AI is so capable that it's tempting to use it for everything. Need to write an email? AI. Need to do math? AI. Need to make a decision? AI.

But skills atrophy when not used. If you stop writing, you get worse at writing. If you stop thinking through problems, you get worse at problem-solving.

**The people who will thrive in an AI-augmented world are those who have real skills of their own** — because:
- They can judge AI output (you can't fact-check what you don't understand)
- They can direct AI more precisely (better prompts require domain knowledge)
- They can do things AI can't (build relationships, make ethical judgments, lead teams, create original art)
- They're valuable when AI fails or is unavailable

### The Balance to Aim For

Use AI to:
- Accelerate tasks you already know how to do
- Explore ideas before developing them yourself
- Get feedback on work you produced
- Handle repetitive or formatting tasks

Don't use AI as a substitute for:
- Developing your own writing voice
- Learning foundational skills in your field
- Critical thinking and judgment
- Building relationships and communicating directly

> Prompt that builds skills, not dependency: "Don't give me the answer. Instead, give me a hint that points me in the right direction, and let me try again."

Use these for reflection or class discussion:

- [ ] If an AI system is biased, who is responsible — the company that built it, the company that uses it, or the person who relies on the output?
- [ ] If a deepfake video of a public figure went viral tomorrow and you suspected it was fake, what would your actual process be to verify it?
- [ ] What skill do you most want to make sure AI doesn't replace in you?

## Key Takeaways

1. Don't share personally identifying information with AI tools — protect your privacy.
2. Deepfakes are real and convincing — verify sources before sharing video or audio.
3. AI bias is systemic, not accidental — it reflects the data it was trained on.
4. Academic integrity with AI is about honest development, not just following rules.
5. Copyright around AI is unsettled — understand the terms of what you use.
6. Build your own skills alongside AI so you remain capable and valuable.

> Prompt to reflect with: "I'm going to describe how I'm currently using AI in my daily life: [describe your usage]. What potential risks or blind spots should I be aware of that I might not have considered?"
