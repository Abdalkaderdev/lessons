# How to Talk to AI
### The single most important skill you'll learn this year

If you only master ONE thing from this entire course, make it this:
**How to write a great prompt.**

Everything else — using AI for school, work, creativity, research — depends on this.

---

## What Even Is a Prompt?

A **prompt** is simply what you type into an AI.

Every time you open [claude.ai](https://claude.ai), [chatgpt.com](https://chatgpt.com), or [gemini.google.com](https://gemini.google.com) and type something, that's a prompt.

The AI doesn't know you. It doesn't know what you want. It only knows what you tell it.

**Bad prompt → mediocre answer.**
**Great prompt → answer that actually helps you.**

Think of it like giving directions. "Go to the store" gets you a confused look. "Drive north on Main Street, turn left at the light, it's the blue building on the right" gets you there.

---

## Why Specific Prompts Beat Vague Ones

Here's the truth: AI models are incredibly powerful, but they're also extremely literal. They answer what you asked, not what you meant.

When your prompt is vague, the AI has to guess:
- Who you are
- What you already know
- What format you want
- How long the answer should be
- What the actual goal is

When your prompt is specific, the AI doesn't have to guess anything. It just delivers.

**The gap between a vague and a specific prompt can be the difference between a useless response and one that saves you hours.**

---

## The CLEAR Framework

Use this framework every time you write a prompt. It works on Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — all of them.

```
C — Context     Who are you? What's the situation?
L — Language    What tone, style, or format do you need?
E — Examples    Show the AI what "good" looks like
A — Audience    Who is this for?
R — Result      What exactly do you want to get?
```

You don't always need all five. But the more you include, the better your result.

## 10+ Real Before/After Prompt Comparisons

### 1. Homework Help

**Vague:**
> Help me with my essay.

**CLEAR:**
> I'm a 9th grader writing a 5-paragraph essay arguing that social media does more harm than good for teenagers. I have my intro and two body paragraphs done. Can you review my argument for logic gaps and suggest a strong third body paragraph point I haven't used yet? Keep your suggestions at a high school reading level.

**Why it works:** The AI knows your grade, your topic, your position, what you've done, and what you need next.

---

### 2. Business Email

**Vague:**
> Write an email to my client.

**CLEAR:**
> Write a professional but friendly email to a client named Sarah who runs a small bakery. I'm a freelance web designer. The project deadline was yesterday and I'm running 3 days behind because I got sick. I want to apologize, give her a new deadline of Friday, and offer her a 10% discount on the final invoice as a goodwill gesture. Keep it under 150 words.

**Why it works:** Every blank is filled in. The AI can write the exact email without guessing.

---

### 3. Creative Writing

**Vague:**
> Write me a short story.

**CLEAR:**
> Write a 400-word short story in the style of a thriller. The main character is a 16-year-old girl who discovers her school's AI system has been logging students' private conversations. The story should end on a cliffhanger. Use short, punchy sentences to keep the tension high.

**Why it works:** Genre, length, character, plot, ending style, sentence structure — all specified.

---

### 4. Research Summary

**Vague:**
> Tell me about climate change.

**CLEAR:**
> Give me a 5-bullet summary of the most important climate change developments from 2020–2025 that a 14-year-old could understand. Focus on facts, not opinions. Include at least one surprising statistic.

**Why it works:** Scope is narrowed, format is specified, audience is defined, there's a specific "hook" requested.

---

### 5. Learning Something New

**Vague:**
> Explain coding to me.

**CLEAR:**
> I'm 12 years old and have never coded before. Explain what Python is and why people use it, using analogies to things a kid would understand (like video games or LEGO). Then give me one simple thing I could type right now to see it work.

**Why it works:** Age, experience level, learning style preference, and a concrete next step are all included.

---

### 6. Making a Decision

**Vague:**
> Should I start a YouTube channel?

**CLEAR:**
> I'm 17 and thinking about starting a YouTube channel about sneaker reviews. I have a decent phone camera, about 5 hours per week free, and I'm comfortable on camera. I'm not expecting to get famous — I just want to build a creative hobby and maybe make a little money eventually. List 3 reasons I should do it and 3 reasons I might want to wait, then give me your honest recommendation.

**Why it works:** Gives the AI actual data to work with instead of asking for generic advice.

---

### 7. Summarizing an Article

**Vague:**
> Summarize this.

**CLEAR:**
> Summarize the following article in exactly 3 sentences. The first sentence should state the main point. The second should give the most important supporting detail. The third should explain why this matters. Write for someone who hasn't read the article. [paste article here]

**Why it works:** The exact format and structure are pre-defined, so the output is immediately usable.

---

### 8. Brainstorming

**Vague:**
> Give me some ideas.

**CLEAR:**
> I'm planning a birthday party for my 13-year-old brother who loves Minecraft and horror movies. Budget is about $150. It'll be at our house with 8 friends. Give me 10 creative theme ideas that mix both interests, ranked from easiest to hardest to set up.

**Why it works:** Interests, constraints, scale, and output format are all clear.

---

### 9. Feedback on Your Work

**Vague:**
> Is this good?

**CLEAR:**
> Read this paragraph I wrote for my history class and give me specific feedback. Focus on: (1) whether my argument is clear, (2) if my evidence actually supports my claim, and (3) one thing I could cut to make it tighter. Be direct — don't just tell me it's great.

**Why it works:** You've told the AI exactly what to look for and signaled you want honest feedback, not flattery.

---

### 10. Debugging a Problem

**Vague:**
> My code doesn't work.

**CLEAR:**
> I'm learning Python and this code is supposed to print numbers 1 to 10, but it only prints 1. I'm using Python 3.11. Here's my code: [paste code]. What's wrong, explain it like I'm a beginner, and show me the fixed version.

**Why it works:** Language version, expected behavior, actual behavior, experience level — all specified.

---

### 11. Analyzing Data

**Vague:**
> Look at this spreadsheet data.

**CLEAR:**
> Here's data from my lemonade stand for the past two weeks: [paste data]. Identify the day and time I made the most sales, the slowest day, and give me two actionable suggestions to increase revenue next week. Present findings as bullet points.

**Why it works:** The AI knows what to look for and how to present it.

---

### 12. Creating Content for Social Media

**Vague:**
> Write an Instagram caption.

**CLEAR:**
> Write 3 Instagram caption options for a photo of me at the beach at sunset. My account is about travel and adventure for teens. Keep each caption under 50 words. Make one funny, one inspirational, and one mysterious. Include 5 relevant hashtags at the end of each.

**Why it works:** Platform, subject, account style, length, tone variety, and format extras are all defined.

## The Prompt Quality Checklist

Before hitting send, ask yourself:

- [ ] Have I told the AI who I am or what my situation is?
- [ ] Have I specified how long the answer should be?
- [ ] Have I said what format I want (list, paragraph, table, etc.)?
- [ ] Have I mentioned who this is for (audience)?
- [ ] Have I been specific about the actual outcome I need?
- [ ] Would a stranger understand exactly what I want from this prompt?

If you can check all six, your prompt is ready.

## Common Beginner Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Being too polite and vague**
"Could you maybe help me perhaps write something about dogs if you don't mind?"
Just say what you want: "Write a 200-word paragraph about why dogs make great pets for apartment living."

**Mistake 2: Giving no constraints**
Without constraints (length, format, tone), the AI makes guesses. Those guesses might not match what's in your head.

**Mistake 3: Asking too many things at once**
Break complex requests into steps. One focused task per prompt usually works better than five tasks crammed together.

**Mistake 4: Not telling the AI it's wrong**
If the first response isn't right, don't start over — just correct it. "That's too formal, rewrite it casually" or "Make it shorter" works great.

**Mistake 5: Forgetting the AI has no memory of you**
Every new chat starts fresh. Re-introduce your context each time, or paste in relevant background.

## Key Takeaways

1. A prompt is just what you type — but HOW you type it changes everything.
2. Specific always beats vague.
3. Use CLEAR: Context, Language, Examples, Audience, Result.
4. If the first answer isn't what you wanted, refine the prompt — don't give up.
5. This skill compounds. The better you get at prompting, the faster you learn everything else.

> Prompt to try right now: "You are a prompt engineering tutor. I'm going to give you a vague prompt, and you will rewrite it using the CLEAR framework (Context, Language, Examples, Audience, Result). Then explain what you changed and why. Ready? Here's my vague prompt: [type anything here]"
