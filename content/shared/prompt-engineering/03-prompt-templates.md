# Prompt Templates You'll Use Forever
### Copy-paste these and fill in the blanks

Stop starting from scratch every time. These templates are designed to work across every major AI tool — [claude.ai](https://claude.ai), [chatgpt.com](https://chatgpt.com), and [gemini.google.com](https://gemini.google.com).

Each one is a proven structure. You just fill in the blanks.

---

## How to Use These Templates

1. Find the template that matches your task
2. Copy the entire block
3. Replace everything in `[brackets]` with your specifics
4. Paste into your AI of choice and hit send
5. Iterate if needed (use the tweak suggestions at the bottom of each template)

**Save this page.** You'll come back to it constantly.

## Section 1 — Research Templates

---

### Template R1: Deep Research Summary

```
I need to understand [TOPIC] for [PURPOSE — e.g., a school presentation, a business decision, personal curiosity].

Please structure your response as:
1. The core concept in 2-3 sentences (explain it to a [YOUR LEVEL — beginner/intermediate/expert])
2. Why it matters — the real-world impact in bullet points
3. The 3 most important things to know about it
4. One surprising or counterintuitive fact
5. Where I should go to learn more (specific books, websites, or topics to search)

Keep the whole response under 500 words.
```

**Tweak:** Change "under 500 words" to however much detail you need. Add "Focus especially on [subtopic]" to narrow it.

---

### Template R2: Compare Two Things

```
Compare [THING A] and [THING B] for someone who is [YOUR CONTEXT — e.g., "a high school student deciding between two colleges"].

Format as a side-by-side table with these rows:
- Main purpose / what it's best for
- Cost (if applicable)
- Pros
- Cons
- Who it's ideal for
- Who should avoid it

After the table, give me your honest recommendation based on my context.
```

---

### Template R3: Research Question Breakdown

```
I'm researching the question: "[YOUR RESEARCH QUESTION]"

I'm at [YOUR LEVEL — beginner, intermediate, advanced] on this topic.

Please:
1. Break this question down into 4-5 smaller sub-questions I should investigate
2. For each sub-question, tell me where to find the best information (specific sources, not just "Google")
3. Flag any part of this topic where the "common knowledge" answer is actually wrong or oversimplified
4. Tell me what experts currently disagree about regarding this topic
```

---

### Template R4: Find the Counterargument

```
I believe: [YOUR POSITION]

Play devil's advocate with full commitment. Give me the strongest possible counterargument — don't hold back. Cite real examples, statistics, or logical challenges where you can.

After making the case against my position, tell me: what's the strongest version of my original position that survives your critique?
```

## Section 2 — Summarizing Templates

---

### Template S1: Summarize Any Text

```
Summarize the following [article/chapter/report/video transcript] in [FORMAT — e.g., 3 bullet points / a single paragraph / a structured outline].

My goal is: [WHY YOU NEED THIS — e.g., "to quickly understand the main argument" / "to share with my team" / "to study for a test"]

Audience: [WHO WILL READ THIS — e.g., me, a classmate, my boss]

Here's the text:
[PASTE TEXT HERE]
```

---

### Template S2: Book/Article Takeaways

```
I just [read/watched/listened to] "[TITLE]" by [AUTHOR/CREATOR].

Give me:
- The 3 biggest ideas I should remember
- The single most actionable takeaway
- One quote or passage worth saving (if possible)
- What type of person would benefit most from this

Don't summarize the plot or structure — just extract what's actually useful.
```

---

### Template S3: Meeting Notes → Action Items

```
Below are raw notes from a [meeting/lecture/call]. Clean them up and extract:

1. Key decisions made
2. Action items (format: who, what, by when — use "TBD" if unknown)
3. Open questions that still need answers
4. A 2-sentence summary of what this meeting was about

Notes:
[PASTE NOTES HERE]
```

## Section 3 — Writing Email Templates

---

### Template E1: Professional Email

```
Write a professional email with these details:

To: [RECIPIENT NAME] — [their role/relationship to me]
From: [YOUR NAME] — [your role]
Subject: [TOPIC]
Goal: [WHAT YOU WANT TO HAPPEN — e.g., schedule a meeting, follow up on a proposal, ask for an extension]
Tone: [TONE — e.g., formal, warm, direct]
Key points to include:
- [POINT 1]
- [POINT 2]
- [POINT 3]
Length: [SHORT under 100 words / MEDIUM 100-200 words / LONG 200+ words]

End with a clear call to action.
```

---

### Template E2: Difficult Message (Apologize, Decline, Deliver Bad News)

```
Help me write a [APOLOGY / DECLINE / DIFFICULT MESSAGE] to [RECIPIENT].

Situation: [EXPLAIN WHAT HAPPENED]
What I want them to understand: [YOUR MAIN POINT]
What I want them to do next (if anything): [DESIRED OUTCOME]
Tone: Honest but not cold. Take responsibility without being overly self-flagellating.
Length: Under 150 words.

Write 2 versions — one more formal, one more personal — so I can choose.
```

---

### Template E3: Follow-Up Email

```
Write a follow-up email for this situation:

I previously [WHAT YOU DID — e.g., applied for, submitted, sent, attended]
It's been [TIME PERIOD] since then
I haven't heard back
I want to: [GOAL — e.g., check on the status, express continued interest, gently nudge]

Keep it short (under 80 words), confident but not pushy, and end with a specific question so they know what to reply to.
```

## Section 4 — Creating Content Templates

---

### Template C1: Social Media Post

```
Write [NUMBER] social media posts for [PLATFORM — Instagram / TikTok caption / Twitter/X / LinkedIn].

Topic/subject: [WHAT IT'S ABOUT]
Brand/personal voice: [DESCRIBE TONE — e.g., casual and funny, professional but approachable, bold and direct]
Target audience: [WHO FOLLOWS YOU]
Goal of the post: [AWARENESS / ENGAGEMENT / PROMOTION / EDUCATION]
Include: [HASHTAGS yes/no] [CALL TO ACTION yes/no] [EMOJI yes/no]
Length limit: [CHARACTER OR WORD COUNT]
```

---

### Template C2: Blog Post Outline

```
Create a detailed outline for a blog post titled: "[WORKING TITLE]"

Target reader: [DESCRIBE YOUR AUDIENCE]
Word count goal: [LENGTH]
Tone: [TONE]
Main argument/takeaway: [WHAT READERS SHOULD BELIEVE OR DO AFTER READING]

For each section include:
- Section heading
- 2-3 bullet points of what to cover
- Any specific examples, stats, or stories to include

Also suggest: a better title if you have one, and a compelling first sentence to hook the reader.
```

---

### Template C3: Script or Talking Points

```
Write a [SCRIPT / TALKING POINTS / OUTLINE] for a [LENGTH — e.g., 3-minute] [FORMAT — e.g., YouTube video / class presentation / podcast intro] about [TOPIC].

Audience: [WHO IS WATCHING/LISTENING]
My goal: [WHAT I WANT THEM TO THINK, FEEL, OR DO]
My style: [HOW I NATURALLY TALK — casual, formal, energetic, calm]
Include: A strong opening hook, [NUMBER] main points, and a clear closing line.

Write it as if I'm speaking out loud — natural sentences, not corporate language.
```

## Section 5 — Analyzing Data Templates

---

### Template D1: Make Sense of Numbers

```
Here is some data I need to understand: [PASTE DATA OR DESCRIBE IT]

Please:
1. Tell me what the data is actually showing in plain language
2. Identify the most significant pattern or trend
3. Flag anything that looks unusual or surprising
4. Give me 2-3 questions this data raises that I should investigate further
5. Suggest one simple visualization that would communicate this well (e.g., bar chart, line graph, pie chart)

I am [NOT / SOMEWHAT / VERY] comfortable with data analysis.
```

---

### Template D2: Turn Data Into a Story

```
I have this data: [PASTE DATA OR DESCRIBE IT]

My audience is: [WHO WILL SEE THIS]
My goal is to convince them that: [YOUR MAIN POINT]

Reframe this data as a narrative — a story with a beginning (here's where we were), middle (here's what changed), and end (here's what it means). Use the numbers as evidence, not as the main character. Keep it under 200 words.
```

## Section 6 — Brainstorming Templates

---

### Template B1: Idea Generator

```
Brainstorm [NUMBER] ideas for [WHAT YOU NEED IDEAS FOR].

Constraints:
- [CONSTRAINT 1 — e.g., budget, time, audience age]
- [CONSTRAINT 2]
- [CONSTRAINT 3]

Give me a range from the most obvious/safe ideas to the most unexpected/unconventional ones. Label each as SAFE, CREATIVE, or WILD.

Don't explain the ideas yet — just list them. I'll ask for details on the ones I like.
```

---

### Template B2: Problem-Solving Brainstorm

```
I'm facing this problem: [DESCRIBE YOUR PROBLEM]

My constraints are: [TIME / BUDGET / RESOURCES / SKILLS you have or don't have]

Generate solutions in three categories:
1. QUICK FIXES — things I could do today
2. REAL SOLUTIONS — things that fix the root cause (take 1-4 weeks)
3. AMBITIOUS MOVES — things that require significant effort but would permanently solve this

For each solution, rate it: effort needed (Low/Medium/High) and likely impact (Low/Medium/High).
```

---

### Template B3: Creative Brainstorm (Names, Titles, Taglines)

```
Generate [NUMBER] options for [A NAME / A TITLE / A TAGLINE / A SLOGAN] for [WHAT IT'S FOR].

About it: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
Audience: [WHO IT'S FOR]
Feeling it should create: [E.G., trustworthy, exciting, mysterious, playful]
Length: [SHORT under 3 words / MEDIUM 3-6 words / FLEXIBLE]

Include a mix of: straightforward, clever/punny, abstract/evocative, and action-oriented options.
```

## Section 7 — Learning New Topics Templates

---

### Template L1: Learn Anything from Scratch

```
Teach me [TOPIC/SKILL] from complete beginner level.

My background: [WHAT YOU ALREADY KNOW RELATED TO THIS]
My goal: [WHAT YOU WANT TO BE ABLE TO DO WITH THIS KNOWLEDGE]
My learning style: [EXAMPLES AND ANALOGIES / STEP-BY-STEP INSTRUCTIONS / THEORY FIRST THEN PRACTICE / JUST SHOW ME HOW]
Time I can commit: [e.g., 30 minutes per day for 2 weeks]

Create a structured learning path. For each stage, tell me: what to learn, the best free resource to use, and how I'll know I've got it before moving on.
```

---

### Template L2: Explain It Different Ways

```
Explain [CONCEPT] to me.

First: in one sentence.
Then: as if I'm 10 years old, using a simple analogy.
Then: at a high school level with actual details.
Then: at an expert level — what do professionals know about this that most people don't?

After all four explanations, ask me which one was closest to my current level so you can pitch future explanations correctly.
```

---

### Template L3: Teach Me by Quizzing

```
I'm trying to learn [TOPIC]. Instead of just explaining it, teach me through questions.

Start with an easy question. If I get it right, ask a harder one. If I get it wrong, explain the concept briefly, then ask the same type of question again using a different example.

Keep going until I've answered 10 questions correctly. Track my score as you go.

Begin when ready.
```

## Section 8 — Getting Feedback Templates

---

### Template F1: Feedback on Writing

```
Give me feedback on this [ESSAY / EMAIL / REPORT / STORY / PARAGRAPH]:

[PASTE YOUR WRITING]

Context:
- Purpose: [WHAT THIS WRITING IS FOR]
- Audience: [WHO WILL READ IT]
- Stage: [FIRST DRAFT / ALMOST DONE / JUST POLISHING]

Focus your feedback on:
1. Is the main message/argument clear? (yes/no + why)
2. What's the weakest sentence or paragraph? (quote it and say why)
3. One structural suggestion (organization, flow, order)
4. One specific thing to cut or simplify

Don't tell me it's "good overall." Be specific and direct.
```

---

### Template F2: Stress Test an Idea

```
Here's my idea: [DESCRIBE YOUR IDEA]

Be a harsh critic. Find every flaw, weakness, and assumption I'm making. Ask the questions a skeptic would ask. Poke holes.

Then — after you've done your worst — tell me what's actually strong about the idea and worth keeping.

Rate the idea overall: 1-10, with honest reasoning.
```

## Section 9 — Debugging Problems Templates

---

### Template P1: Debug Any Problem

```
I'm trying to [WHAT YOU'RE TRYING TO DO] but [WHAT'S GOING WRONG].

Here's what I know:
- What I expected to happen: [EXPECTED]
- What actually happened: [ACTUAL]
- What I've already tried: [ATTEMPTS]
- Relevant details: [ANY CODE, ERROR MESSAGES, SCREENSHOTS, OR CONTEXT]

Think step by step through what might be causing this. Start with the most likely cause. For each cause, tell me how to confirm it and how to fix it.
```

---

### Template P2: Explain an Error Message

```
I got this error: [PASTE ERROR MESSAGE]

I'm using: [TOOL / APP / LANGUAGE / PLATFORM]
My experience level: [BEGINNER / INTERMEDIATE / ADVANCED]
What I was doing when it happened: [CONTEXT]

Explain:
1. What this error actually means in plain language
2. The most common reason it happens
3. Step-by-step how to fix it
4. How to prevent it next time
```

## Section 10 — Making Decisions Templates

---

### Template M1: Decision Framework

```
I need to make a decision between:
Option A: [DESCRIBE A]
Option B: [DESCRIBE B]
Option C: [DESCRIBE C — delete this line if only two options]

My priorities (rank these for me if needed):
- [PRIORITY 1]
- [PRIORITY 2]
- [PRIORITY 3]

My constraints: [TIME / MONEY / SKILLS / OTHER LIMITS]

Walk me through a structured decision framework. Score each option against my priorities. Give me your recommendation with clear reasoning. Don't just list pros and cons — actually make a call.
```

---

### Template M2: Pre-Mortem (Spot Failure Before It Happens)

```
I'm about to [DO SOMETHING / START A PROJECT / MAKE A DECISION]: [DESCRIBE IT]

My plan is: [BRIEF PLAN]

Do a pre-mortem: Imagine it's [6 months / 1 year] from now and this completely failed. What went wrong? List the top 5 most likely failure modes.

For each one:
- Probability: Low / Medium / High
- Warning sign I should watch for
- How I could prevent it or reduce the risk right now
```

## Build Your Personal Template Library

Don't just use these templates once. Build your own library:

1. When a prompt works really well, save it somewhere (Notion, Notes app, a Google Doc)
2. Add a short label so future-you knows when to use it
3. Refine it every time you use it — make it better with each run
4. Share your best ones with classmates

**The students who build a personal prompt library are the ones who get dramatically faster at everything they do with AI.**

## Key Takeaways

1. Templates save time — you never start from scratch.
2. The blank-filling process forces you to clarify what you actually need.
3. Combining templates unlocks even more power.
4. Your best prompts are worth saving and reusing.
5. Templates are starting points — always tweak them for your specific situation.
