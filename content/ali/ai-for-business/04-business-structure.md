# Business Structure & Operations

## What You'll Learn

- How to use AI to map out your organization structure
- How to create Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for any business task
- How to document workflows and processes clearly
- How to use AI for process optimization
- How to write employee handbooks and onboarding materials
- How to run more productive meetings with AI-assisted agendas and minutes

> Operational clarity is what separates a real business from a chaotic side hustle. This lesson gives you the tools to build that clarity — without hiring a consultant.

---

## Why Operations Matter

A business that depends entirely on the founder knowing everything in their head is fragile. What happens when you hire your first employee? When you're sick? When you want to take a vacation?

Operations documentation means:
- Anyone can understand how things work
- New employees can get up to speed faster
- You can spot inefficiencies and fix them
- You look credible to investors, partners, and clients

AI makes creating this documentation fast — so there's no excuse to skip it.

## Part 1: Organizational Charts

### What Is an Org Chart?

An org chart is a visual map of who reports to whom in an organization. Even as a one-person business, it helps you see all the "roles" that need to be filled — whether by you, a contractor, or a future hire.

### Designing Your Org Structure with AI

> Prompt: "I'm building a small [type of business] with [current team size] people. The business functions that need to be covered are: [list them — e.g., sales, operations, marketing, finance, customer service, product development]. Help me design an organizational structure appropriate for this stage. Who should report to whom? What roles should be combined at this stage vs. separated later as we grow?"

> Prompt: "Create a text-based org chart for my business with the following structure: [paste the structure AI suggested or your own]. Show it as a hierarchy with clear reporting lines."

### Roles vs. People

A key concept: **roles** are the functions your business needs. **People** fill those roles. At the start, you might fill 5 roles yourself. That's fine — but knowing what those 5 roles are helps you think about delegation and hiring.

> Prompt: "Based on the org structure we designed, list all the key responsibilities under each role. For each responsibility, note whether it requires specialized skill, can be delegated to a generalist, or could eventually be automated."

## Part 2: Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)

### What Is an SOP?

An SOP is a step-by-step document that describes how to perform a specific, recurring task. Think of it as a recipe — anyone who follows it should get the same result.

**Examples of tasks that need SOPs:**
- How to onboard a new client
- How to handle a customer complaint
- How to process an order
- How to create a weekly social media post
- How to prepare a monthly financial report

### Why SOPs Are Valuable

- You stop reinventing the wheel every time a task comes up
- You can hand off tasks to others confidently
- You can audit your own processes and find inefficiencies
- They're required if you ever want to franchise or scale

### Creating an SOP with AI

> Prompt: "Write a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for the following task: [describe the task in detail]. The person following this SOP has basic computer skills but no specific background in this area. Include: purpose of the SOP, when it should be used, materials/tools needed, step-by-step instructions (numbered), common mistakes to avoid, and how to verify the task was completed correctly."

**Example:**

> Prompt: "Write an SOP for onboarding a new freelance client for a social media management agency. Include all steps from initial inquiry through to first content delivery. The person following this may be a junior team member."

### SOP Template Structure

A good SOP includes:
- **Title** — What process this covers
- **Purpose** — Why this process exists
- **Scope** — Who this applies to, when it's used
- **Responsibilities** — Who is accountable
- **Materials/Tools** — What you need
- **Procedure** — Numbered steps
- **Quality Check** — How to verify it was done right
- **Version & Review Date** — SOPs should be reviewed periodically

> Prompt: "Format this procedure as a proper SOP document using the standard template structure: Title, Purpose, Scope, Responsibilities, Materials, Procedure (numbered steps), Quality Checks, and version information."

## Part 3: Workflow Documentation

### SOPs vs. Workflows

| SOP | Workflow |
|-----|---------|
| Describes a single task | Describes a multi-step process with decisions |
| Linear steps | May have branches (if X, then Y) |
| One person usually | Often involves multiple people/departments |

A workflow shows the entire journey of a process — including decision points, handoffs between team members, and what happens if something goes wrong.

### Mapping a Workflow with AI

> Prompt: "Map out the complete workflow for [describe the business process — e.g., receiving a customer order, handling a refund, onboarding a new employee]. Include all steps from start to finish, decision points (where the path changes based on a condition), who is responsible for each step, and what tools or systems are used. Present it as a numbered process map."

> Prompt: "Now describe this same workflow as if explaining it for a flowchart. Use plain language to describe each box and decision diamond."

### Identifying Bottlenecks

> Prompt: "Based on this workflow, where are the most likely bottlenecks or failure points? For each one, suggest a way to streamline or eliminate the issue."

## Part 4: Process Optimization

Once you have your workflows documented, you can use AI to make them better.

### The LEAN Approach

LEAN thinking comes from manufacturing but applies everywhere. The goal: eliminate waste. Waste means any step that doesn't add value for the customer.

> Prompt: "Review this workflow for my [business process]. Apply LEAN principles to identify any steps that are wasteful, redundant, or could be automated. Suggest a revised, more efficient version of the workflow."

### Automation Opportunities

> Prompt: "In this workflow, which steps could be automated using tools like Zapier, Make.com, or AI assistants? Which steps require human judgment and cannot be automated? Give me a priority list for automation."

### Measuring Process Quality

> Prompt: "What KPIs (key performance indicators) should I track for this process? What is a reasonable benchmark for each? How would I know if the process is performing well or poorly?"

- [ ] Ask AI to apply LEAN principles and produce an optimized version.
- [ ] Identify 1 step that could be automated and research which tool you'd use to do it.

## Part 5: Employee Handbooks

### What Goes in an Employee Handbook?

An employee handbook sets expectations before a new hire starts. It typically covers:

1. Welcome message and company overview
2. Mission, vision, and values
3. Code of conduct and professional standards
4. Working hours, attendance, and leave policies
5. Compensation and benefits overview
6. Performance review process
7. Communication guidelines (internal tools, response times)
8. Confidentiality and data handling
9. Disciplinary procedures
10. Health, safety, and emergency procedures

### Creating a Handbook with AI

> Prompt: "Create an employee handbook for a small [type of business] with [number] employees. The company values are [list 3–5 values]. Include sections on: welcome message, our mission and values, code of conduct, working hours, leave and time-off policy, communication standards, confidentiality, performance expectations, and what happens if policies are not followed. Keep the tone professional but human — we want people to feel welcomed, not overwhelmed."

> Prompt: "Write a welcome letter for the beginning of the handbook — something warm and personal that a new employee would be genuinely glad to receive on their first day."

## Part 6: Meeting Agendas and Minutes

### Why Most Meetings Fail

Most meetings fail because:
- No clear agenda was set in advance
- People aren't sure what decisions need to be made
- No one captures action items
- There's no follow-up

AI fixes all of this.

### Creating a Meeting Agenda

> Prompt: "Create a meeting agenda for a [type of meeting — e.g., weekly team check-in, client project review, quarterly strategy review]. The main topics to cover are: [list topics]. The meeting is [duration] long. Format it with time allocations for each item, who is responsible for presenting each item, and the desired outcome of each discussion (decision, information share, or brainstorm)."

### Taking and Formatting Meeting Minutes

After a meeting, you can paste rough notes and ask AI to format them properly:

> Prompt: "Here are my rough notes from a business meeting. Convert them into professional meeting minutes with the following format: Date, Attendees, Agenda Items (with discussion summary), Decisions Made, Action Items (with owner and deadline), and Next Meeting Date. Notes: [paste your rough notes]"

> Prompt: "Write a follow-up email to send to all meeting attendees summarizing the decisions made and action items. Keep it brief and clear."
