# Business Plans & Strategy

## What You'll Learn

- What a business plan actually is and when you need one
- How to draft a complete business plan with AI
- How to write a compelling executive summary and mission/vision statement
- How to build basic financial projections with AI help
- How to create a pitch deck outline that investors and partners will take seriously

> By the end of this lesson, you will have a first draft of a real business plan for your idea.

---

## Why Business Plans Still Matter

You might have heard "business plans are dead" or "just start and figure it out." That advice works for some side hustles — but if you ever want a bank loan, an investor, a business partner, or even a serious employee, you need a document that shows you've thought this through.

A business plan also forces you to think clearly. It's easy to have a vague idea that sounds good in your head. Writing it out — with numbers, timelines, and strategy — reveals the gaps.

The good news: AI makes writing one dramatically faster. What used to take weeks now takes hours.

---

## Part 1: The Structure of a Business Plan

A standard business plan has these sections:

| Section | What It Covers |
|---------|---------------|
| Executive Summary | The whole plan in 1 page |
| Company Overview | What you do, your mission, your legal structure |
| Market Analysis | Research on your industry, customers, competitors |
| Products & Services | What you're selling and why it's valuable |
| Marketing & Sales Strategy | How you'll find and convert customers |
| Operations Plan | How the business actually runs day-to-day |
| Management Team | Who is involved and what they bring |
| Financial Projections | Revenue, costs, and profitability forecasts |
| Appendix | Supporting documents, data, references |

You don't always need all of these. A lean plan for a small business might be 5–8 pages. A plan for an investor pitch might be 20+. Start lean, then expand.

## Part 2: Drafting Your Business Plan with AI

### Starting the Draft

The best approach is section by section. Don't ask AI to write the whole plan at once — the result will be generic. Instead, feed it information about your specific idea and ask for each section.

> Prompt: "I'm writing a business plan for [your business idea]. Here's what I know so far: [paste your market research summary, customer persona, SWOT]. Help me draft the Company Overview section. Include: what the business does, the problem it solves, and the value it provides. Keep it professional and specific."

Work through each section this way. After each draft, refine with follow-up prompts:

> Prompt: "Make this more concise." / "Add a sentence about our competitive advantage." / "Make the tone more confident."

### The Products & Services Section

This is where you describe what you're selling in a way that makes the value obvious.

> Prompt: "Help me write the Products & Services section of my business plan. My business is [describe it]. Explain what I offer, how it works, the pricing model I'm considering, and why customers will prefer it over alternatives."

### The Marketing & Sales Strategy Section

> Prompt: "Write a Marketing & Sales Strategy section for my business plan. My target customer is [persona]. My budget is limited. Focus on low-cost, high-impact channels like [social media, word of mouth, partnerships, etc.]. Include a brief customer acquisition plan."

- [ ] Draft at least 3 sections of your business plan using the prompts above.
- [ ] Review each section: does it sound like your actual business, or is it too generic? If too generic, add more specific details and regenerate.

## Part 3: Executive Summaries

The executive summary is the most important page in your business plan. It's written last but appears first. Investors and partners often decide whether to read the rest based solely on this page.

### What It Must Include

1. What your business does (1–2 sentences)
2. The problem you solve and for whom
3. Your solution and why it's better
4. Market size (a key number)
5. Revenue model (how you make money)
6. Current status (what stage you're at)
7. What you need (funding, partnership, etc.) — if applicable

### How to Write It with AI

> Prompt: "Write a one-page executive summary for my business plan. My business is [name], which [describe what you do]. We target [customer persona]. The market is worth approximately [size]. Our revenue model is [subscription/product sales/service fees/etc.]. We are currently at [idea stage/pre-launch/launched]. Write it in a confident, professional tone."

> Prompt (refinement): "The executive summary is good but I want the opening sentence to be more compelling — something that immediately communicates the problem and opportunity. Can you rewrite just the opening?"

- [ ] Read it aloud. Does it sound like something you'd be proud to hand to a potential investor?
- [ ] Identify one sentence that's weak and improve it with a follow-up prompt.

## Part 4: Mission and Vision Statements

### The Difference

- **Mission statement** — What you do, for whom, and why. Present tense.
- **Vision statement** — Where you're going in the long term. Future tense.

**Examples:**

| Company | Mission | Vision |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Tesla | "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy." | "To create the most compelling car company of the 21st century." |
| A small tutoring business | "To help university students achieve academic success through personalized, affordable coaching." | "To become the leading student support platform in the Gulf region." |

### Writing Yours with AI

> Prompt: "Help me write a mission statement and a vision statement for my business. My business: [describe]. My core customer: [describe]. My long-term ambition: [describe]. Keep each statement under 2 sentences. They should be inspiring but realistic."

> Prompt: "Give me 3 variations of the mission statement — one formal, one conversational, one bold. I'll choose which tone fits best."

- [ ] Pick your favorite — or combine elements from multiple versions.
- [ ] Write your vision statement.

## Part 5: Financial Projections

### What Are Financial Projections?

Financial projections are estimates of your revenue, costs, and profit over a set period — usually 1–3 years. They're not a guarantee; they're your best educated guess based on real assumptions.

### The Key Components

- **Revenue forecast** — How many units/clients/subscribers you expect and at what price
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** — Direct costs to deliver your product/service
- **Operating expenses** — Rent, salaries, software, marketing
- **Gross profit** — Revenue minus COGS
- **Net profit** — Revenue minus all expenses

### Using AI for Financial Projections

AI won't magically know your numbers — but it can help you build the model and think through your assumptions.

> Prompt: "Help me build a simple 12-month revenue projection for my business. My product/service is [describe]. My planned price is [amount]. I expect to start with [X] customers in month 1 and grow by approximately [Y%] per month. Build me a table showing projected monthly revenue, estimated costs (I'll fill in the specifics), and monthly profit/loss."

Once you have the model:

> Prompt: "What assumptions am I making in this financial model that I should test or validate before launching? What are the most optimistic assumptions that could hurt me if they're wrong?"

### Understanding Break-Even

Break-even is the point at which your revenue covers your costs — you're no longer losing money.

> Prompt: "My fixed monthly costs are approximately [amount]. My product sells for [price] and costs me [COGS] to deliver. Calculate my break-even point in units per month and in months from launch (assuming steady growth)."

- [ ] Identify your top 3 financial assumptions (e.g., "I assume I'll get 10 new customers per month").
- [ ] For each assumption, write a one-sentence justification for why that number is realistic.

## Part 6: Pitch Deck Outline

A pitch deck is a visual summary of your business plan — typically 10–15 slides used to present to investors, partners, or even potential clients.

### The Classic Structure (Guy Kawasaki's 10-Slide Rule)

1. Title / Company Name
2. Problem
3. Solution
4. Business Model (How You Make Money)
5. Underlying Magic (Your Unique Advantage)
6. Marketing & Sales
7. Competition
8. Team
9. Financial Projections
10. Current Status & Ask

### Creating a Pitch Deck Outline with AI

> Prompt: "Create a 10-slide pitch deck outline for my business [name]. My business [describe]. Include: the key point for each slide, 2–3 bullet points of content per slide, and a suggested visual or data point to include. Make it compelling for an early-stage investor."

> Prompt: "Write the speaker notes for slide 3 (the Solution slide). Keep it under 60 seconds when read aloud. It should clearly explain what we do and why it's better than the status quo."

## Putting It All Together

You now have the building blocks of a real business plan:
- A structured document with key sections drafted
- A compelling executive summary
- Mission and vision statements
- Basic financial projections
- A pitch deck outline

This is more than most people ever do before starting a business. The difference between you and someone who just "has an idea" is that you've thought it through.
