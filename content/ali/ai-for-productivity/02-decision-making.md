# Decision Making with AI

## What You'll Learn

- How to use AI for structured pros/cons analysis
- How to use decision matrices to evaluate options objectively
- How to assess risk before making business decisions
- How to get AI to play "devil's advocate" and challenge your thinking
- How to use scenario planning to stress-test a decision before committing

> The quality of your life and business is determined by the quality of your decisions. AI can make your decisions better — and faster.

---

## Why Decision Making Is Hard

Humans are naturally bad at making decisions for a few reasons:
- **Confirmation bias** — We favor information that confirms what we already believe
- **Loss aversion** — We fear losses more than we value equivalent gains
- **Recency bias** — We overweight recent events
- **Emotional reasoning** — We decide based on how we feel, then rationalize afterward

AI doesn't have these biases. It can give you a more objective analysis than your own gut — especially for high-stakes decisions.

This doesn't mean ignoring your instincts. It means checking them with a rational framework before committing.

## Part 1: Pros and Cons Analysis

### The Classic Approach — Upgraded

The simple pros/cons list is something most people do informally and badly. AI can make it rigorous.

> Prompt: "I'm deciding whether to [describe the decision]. Help me build a thorough pros and cons analysis. Consider: financial impact, time investment, opportunity cost, risks, potential upsides, and impact on my long-term goals. Don't just list surface-level points — go deep on the most important 3–4 factors on each side."

### Weighting Pros and Cons

Not all pros and cons are equal. Losing AED 5,000 matters more than an aesthetic preference. AI can help you weight them:

> Prompt: "Take the pros and cons we identified and assign each one a weight from 1–10 based on how much it should influence the decision. Then calculate a weighted score for each side. Based on the weighted analysis, what does the rational choice appear to be?"

### The "Pre-Mortem" Exercise

A pre-mortem asks: "Imagine it's 12 months from now and this decision turned out to be a disaster. What went wrong?"

This helps you surface risks you might be ignoring.

> Prompt: "I'm considering [describe the decision]. Run a pre-mortem: assume I make this decision and 12 months later it has failed badly. What are the most likely reasons it failed? Be specific and realistic — not generic. What would I wish I had considered or done differently?"

- [ ] Identify a real decision you're currently facing (business or personal).
- [ ] Run a full pros/cons analysis with weighting.
- [ ] Run the pre-mortem.
- [ ] Do the analysis change your thinking at all? Write a one-paragraph reflection.

## Part 2: Decision Matrices

### What Is a Decision Matrix?

A decision matrix (also called a weighted decision matrix) is a tool for comparing multiple options against multiple criteria — all in a structured table.

**When to use it:** When you have 3+ options and 3+ evaluation criteria. Examples:
- Choosing between 3 business locations
- Deciding which software tool to use
- Comparing job offers
- Evaluating potential business partners

### How a Decision Matrix Works

1. List your options (rows)
2. List your criteria (columns)
3. Assign each criterion a weight (how important is it?)
4. Score each option on each criterion (1–10)
5. Multiply score × weight for each cell
6. Sum the weighted scores for each option
7. The highest total is the "rational" choice

### Building a Decision Matrix with AI

> Prompt: "I need to decide between these options: [list your options]. The criteria I care about are: [list criteria — e.g., cost, time to implement, quality, scalability, alignment with my goals]. Help me build a weighted decision matrix. Ask me to rate the importance of each criterion (1–10), then help me score each option on each criterion. Calculate the weighted totals and tell me what the matrix recommends."

> Prompt: "I'm choosing a location for my business. The options are: [list locations]. The criteria are: rent cost, foot traffic, proximity to target customers, parking availability, and neighborhood growth potential. Build a decision matrix, assign weights based on a retail business's priorities, and score each location based on general assumptions (I'll adjust any scores I disagree with)."

### Challenging the Matrix Result

> Prompt: "The decision matrix recommends Option [X], but my gut says Option [Y]. What are the most common reasons someone's instinct diverges from a rational matrix result? Is there a factor I may have underweighted or left off the matrix entirely?"

- [ ] Choose a business decision with 2–4 options (tool selection, pricing strategy, partnership decision, etc.).
- [ ] Build a full decision matrix with AI.
- [ ] Adjust any scores you disagree with and note why.
- [ ] Does the matrix match your intuition? If not, why not?

## Part 3: Risk Assessment

### What Is Risk Assessment?

Risk assessment means identifying what could go wrong, how likely it is, how bad it would be, and what you can do about it — before you make a decision.

Professional risk assessments are done before every major business decision: a new hire, a big investment, a product launch, a partnership.

### The Risk Assessment Framework

For each identified risk:
- **Description** — What could go wrong?
- **Likelihood** — How likely is this? (Low / Medium / High)
- **Impact** — How bad would this be if it happened? (Low / Medium / High)
- **Risk Score** — Likelihood × Impact (High × High = Critical)
- **Mitigation** — What can you do to reduce the likelihood or impact?
- **Contingency** — If it happens anyway, what's the plan?

### Running a Risk Assessment with AI

> Prompt: "I'm about to [describe a decision or action — e.g., invest AED 20,000 in inventory, hire a full-time employee, sign a 2-year office lease]. Perform a risk assessment. Identify at least 8 specific risks. For each risk, rate likelihood and impact as Low/Medium/High, calculate a risk score, suggest a mitigation strategy, and suggest a contingency plan if it occurs despite mitigation."

> Prompt: "Based on the risk assessment, what is the single highest-priority risk I should address before proceeding? What specific action should I take in the next 7 days to reduce this risk?"

### Risk Tolerance

Different decisions have different risk tolerances. Ask AI to help you calibrate:

> Prompt: "I'm considering [describe decision]. My financial situation is: [describe — e.g., I have 6 months runway, I have no dependents, this is 30% of my savings]. Given my situation, is this an acceptable level of risk? Compare it to typical risk levels for similar decisions at this business stage."

### Exercise 3
- [ ] Choose a significant business decision you'll make in the next 3 months.
- [ ] Run a full risk assessment with at least 6 risks identified.
- [ ] Identify your top 2 risks and write a specific mitigation plan for each.
- [ ] Decide: knowing these risks, do you still want to proceed? What conditions would make you more confident?

## Part 4: Getting a Second Opinion from AI

### AI as a Devil's Advocate

One of the most valuable uses of AI in decision-making is having it challenge your thinking. You describe your plan — and ask AI to tear it apart.

> Prompt: "Here is a decision I've made: [describe]. I believe this is the right choice because: [describe your reasoning]. I want you to play devil's advocate. Challenge every assumption I've made. Point out what I might be missing, what could go wrong, and where my reasoning might be flawed. Don't agree with me — push back hard."

> Prompt: "I'm planning to [describe a business decision]. What would a skeptical, experienced business mentor say about this plan? What are the 3 most likely objections they'd raise? How should I address each objection?"

### The 10-10-10 Framework

Ask yourself: How will I feel about this decision in:
- 10 minutes?
- 10 months?
- 10 years?

> Prompt: "I'm deciding whether to [describe decision]. Apply the 10-10-10 framework. How might I feel about this decision 10 minutes from now (immediate emotional response)? 10 months from now (medium-term impact)? 10 years from now (long-term significance)? Help me understand the time horizon of this decision."

### Asking AI to Validate Your Reasoning

> Prompt: "I want to make sure my reasoning about this decision is sound. Here is my logic: [describe your thinking step by step]. Are there any logical fallacies, incorrect assumptions, or missing considerations in this reasoning? Am I making any common decision-making errors?"

### Exercise 4
- [ ] Take a decision you've already made (past or current).
- [ ] Ask AI to challenge it using the devil's advocate prompt.
- [ ] Identify: was there anything you hadn't considered? Does it change your view?
- [ ] Practice applying the 10-10-10 framework to one current decision.

## Part 5: Scenario Planning

### What Is Scenario Planning?

Scenario planning means thinking through multiple possible futures before making a decision — so you're prepared for what might happen, not just what you hope will happen.

It answers: "What do I do if X happens? What if Y happens instead?"

### The 3 Scenarios Framework

For any significant decision, plan for:
- **Best Case** — Everything goes right, favorable external conditions
- **Base Case** — Realistic expectations, some things go right and wrong
- **Worst Case** — Key assumptions fail, unfavorable conditions

> Prompt: "I'm planning to [describe a business action — e.g., launch a new product, open a second location, hire a sales team]. Build 3 scenarios: best case, base case, and worst case. For each scenario: describe the conditions that create it, the financial outcome, and what my response strategy should be. What are the early warning signs that I'm heading toward the worst case, so I can course-correct early?"

### Stress-Testing a Decision

> Prompt: "I'm planning to invest AED [amount] in [describe]. Stress-test this decision: (1) What is the maximum I could lose? (2) If I lost that amount, what would the impact be on my business/life? (3) Is this a reversible or irreversible decision? (4) Under what circumstances would this be a catastrophic mistake vs. just a setback? (5) What's my exit strategy if this doesn't work?"

### The Reversibility Test

One of the most important questions before any decision:

> "Is this reversible or irreversible?"

Reversible decisions (you can undo them) can be made quickly and experimented with. Irreversible decisions (signing a 3-year lease, having a public falling out, major financial commitment) require more careful analysis.

> Prompt: "Here is a decision I'm considering: [describe]. Is this primarily reversible or irreversible? What would it cost (time, money, reputation) to reverse it if needed? Given this, how much time and analysis should I invest in the decision before committing?"

### Exercise 5
- [ ] Choose a medium-to-large business decision you're currently facing.
- [ ] Build a 3-scenario plan (best, base, worst).
- [ ] Identify the early warning signs for the worst case.
- [ ] Determine: is this decision reversible? How does that affect your timeline for deciding?

## Putting It All Together

You now have a complete decision-making toolkit:
- Pros/cons analysis with proper weighting

Better decisions compound. Making 5% better decisions every day produces dramatically better outcomes over a year.
