# Task & Project Management

## What You'll Learn

- How to use AI to break big projects into manageable tasks
- How to prioritize using the Eisenhower Matrix and other frameworks
- How to plan your week with AI assistance

> The difference between a productive person and an overwhelmed one is usually not talent or time — it's a system. This lesson builds yours.

## The Problem with "Just Working Hard"

Working hard without a system means:
- Doing urgent but unimportant tasks while the important ones sit undone
- Starting projects without a clear plan and losing momentum
- Feeling busy but not making progress on the things that actually matter
- Forgetting tasks, missing deadlines, and dropping balls

A task management system built with AI takes less than an hour to set up and saves you hours every week.

## Part 1: Breaking Down Big Projects

### Why Big Projects Feel Overwhelming

A project like "launch my business website" is overwhelming because it's too vague. Your brain doesn't know what to do with it.

But "write the homepage headline" is concrete. You can do it in 20 minutes.

The skill of breaking a big goal into small, actionable steps is called **task decomposition** — and AI is excellent at it.

### The Project Decomposition Prompt

> Prompt: "I need to complete the following project: [describe the project]. Break it down into a complete task list with every step needed to complete this from start to finish. Organize the tasks in order of sequence (what needs to happen first?). For each task, estimate how long it will take and whether it requires any specific tool, skill, or prerequisite to be completed first."

**Example:**

> Prompt: "I need to launch a freelance social media management business from scratch. Break this down into every task I need to complete before I have my first paying client. Organize by phase (Setup, Launch, First Client). For each task, estimate time required and note any dependencies."

### Phases and Milestones

After breaking down the tasks:

> Prompt: "Take this task list and group the tasks into phases. Each phase should have a milestone — a clear deliverable that signals the phase is complete. Suggest realistic timelines for each phase."

- [ ] Choose a real project you need to complete (launching your business, building your website, landing your first client).
- [ ] Use the decomposition prompt to break it into tasks.
- [ ] Add time estimates to each task.
- [ ] Calculate the total time required — is the timeline realistic?

## Part 2: Priority Frameworks

### Why Prioritization Is a Skill

Most people work from a to-do list. The problem: a to-do list doesn't tell you what to do first. You end up doing easy tasks (replying to messages, organizing files) while avoiding high-impact work (sending that proposal, making that sales call).

Prioritization frameworks give you a rational system for deciding what to do when.

### Framework 1: The Eisenhower Matrix

Divide tasks into 4 quadrants based on urgency and importance:

| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|-|--------|-----------|
| **Important** | DO NOW (Q1) | SCHEDULE (Q2) |
| **Not Important** | DELEGATE (Q3) | DELETE (Q4) |

- **Q1 (Do Now):** Client deadline today, urgent client issue, imminent payment
- **Q2 (Schedule):** Long-term planning, skill development, relationship building — the most important quadrant
- **Q3 (Delegate):** Meetings you don't need to attend, routine requests, admin tasks
- **Q4 (Delete):** Social media scrolling, low-value emails, tasks that don't serve your goals

The insight: most people spend their time in Q1 and Q3 while neglecting Q2 — where the real growth happens.

> Prompt: "Here is my current task list: [paste all your tasks]. Categorize each task into the Eisenhower Matrix (Q1: Urgent+Important, Q2: Not Urgent+Important, Q3: Urgent+Not Important, Q4: Neither). Then give me a prioritized order for today, focusing on Q1 first and Q2 second."

### Framework 2: The MoSCoW Method

Used for projects and product decisions:
- **Must Have** — Non-negotiable, must be done
- **Should Have** — Important but not critical
- **Could Have** — Nice to have
- **Won't Have (this time)** — Explicitly out of scope now

> Prompt: "Apply the MoSCoW framework to my project task list: [paste]. Categorize each task and tell me the minimum viable version of this project — what is the smallest set of 'Must Have' tasks I could complete and still have a meaningful result?"

### Framework 3: The 1-3-5 Rule

Each day, plan:
- 1 big thing
- 3 medium things
- 5 small things

> Prompt: "Based on my current task list [paste], create a 1-3-5 plan for tomorrow. Choose the 1 most impactful task as my big thing. Pick 3 medium tasks that each take 30–60 minutes. Choose 5 small tasks (under 15 minutes each). My available work hours tomorrow: [hours]."

- [ ] Build a 1-3-5 plan for tomorrow.

## Part 3: Weekly Planning with AI

### Why Most People Don't Plan Their Weeks

Planning feels like wasted time — until you try it. A 30-minute Sunday evening planning session saves 5+ hours of decision fatigue and distraction throughout the week.

### The Weekly Planning Process

> Prompt: "Help me plan my work week. My goals this week are: [list 2–3 big goals]. My tasks that must be done this week: [list]. My recurring commitments: [meetings, calls, fixed obligations]. I work [X] hours per day, [days] per week. Build a day-by-day plan that: allocates time blocks to each task, protects the highest-focus work for my best hours (I'm most productive in [morning/afternoon/evening]), and leaves buffer time for unexpected tasks. Present it as a table."

### The Weekly Review

Every Friday (or end of week), do a brief review:

> Prompt: "It's the end of my work week. Here's what I planned to do: [paste your weekly plan]. Here's what I actually completed: [describe]. What didn't get done and why? Help me: (1) identify patterns in what I'm avoiding, (2) reschedule incomplete tasks, and (3) build a better plan for next week that accounts for what I learned about how I actually work."

### Time Blocking

Time blocking means assigning specific time slots to specific types of work — and protecting those slots.

> Prompt: "Based on my work requirements, design an ideal weekly time-block schedule for me. My work types: deep work (client projects, writing, analysis), admin (emails, invoices, scheduling), meetings, learning, and planning. I work 8am–6pm, Monday–Friday, with Friday afternoons sacred for review and planning. Optimize for maximum deep work time during my peak hours (morning) and batch all admin and meetings into the afternoon."

- [ ] Schedule at least 2 "deep work" blocks of 90+ uninterrupted minutes.
- [ ] Schedule a 30-minute Friday review session.
- [ ] At the end of the week, do the weekly review prompt and compare planned vs. actual.

## Part 4: Delegation Frameworks

### Why Delegation Is Hard

Most people either:
1. Don't delegate because "it's faster to do it myself"
2. Delegate poorly — no clear instructions, no follow-up, bad results

Both patterns keep you stuck doing everything yourself.

### The 3 Questions Before Delegating

1. Can this task be done to an acceptable standard by someone else?
2. Is there time to explain it properly (upfront investment pays off)?
3. Is this task taking me away from work only I can do?

If yes to all three — delegate.

### The Delegation Brief

Before handing a task to someone, prepare a brief:

> Prompt: "Help me write a delegation brief for the following task: [describe task]. I want to hand this to [describe the person — a VA, an employee, a freelancer]. The brief should include: task objective, background context, step-by-step instructions, the quality standard I expect, the deadline, how to ask questions if stuck, and how to deliver the completed work. Make it clear enough that this person can complete the task without needing to ask me anything."

### The 70% Rule

If someone can do a task 70% as well as you can — delegate it. Your time on their 30% gap is worth more on higher-value activities.

> Prompt: "I'm trying to decide what tasks to delegate as I start bringing on help. Here is my current task list and how long each takes me per week: [list tasks with time]. Which tasks should I prioritize delegating first? Consider: which are lowest skill, which are most time-consuming, and which take me away from revenue-generating or strategic work."

### Exercise 4
- [ ] Identify 3 tasks in your business that could be delegated.
- [ ] Write a delegation brief for one of them using the prompt above.
- [ ] Ask AI: "If I delegated these 3 tasks, approximately how many hours per week would I free up? What higher-value work could I fill that time with?"

## Part 5: Planning a Real Project with AI

### The Complete Project Planning Process

For any significant project, use this sequence:

**Step 1: Define the project clearly**
> Prompt: "Help me define this project: [describe vaguely]. Ask me clarifying questions until we have: a clear objective, a definition of done (how we know it's complete), the key deliverables, the stakeholders involved, and a rough timeline. Start by asking me 5 questions."

**Step 2: Break it into tasks**
(Use the decomposition prompt from Part 1)

**Step 3: Create a project timeline (Gantt-style)**
> Prompt: "Based on this task list and estimated durations [paste], create a project timeline. Show which tasks can happen in parallel and which depend on others being done first. Present it as a week-by-week schedule, noting key milestones."

**Step 4: Identify risks**
> Prompt: "What are the top 5 risks to this project completing on time and at quality? For each risk, rate its likelihood and impact (High/Medium/Low) and suggest a mitigation plan."

**Step 5: Create a project tracker**
> Prompt: "Create a simple project tracker table I can use in Google Sheets. Columns should include: Task, Owner, Status (Not Started / In Progress / Done / Blocked), Due Date, Priority, and Notes. Populate it with the tasks from my project plan."
