# Reports & Proposals

## What You'll Learn

- How to structure and write business reports with AI
- How to write project proposals that win business
- How to write case studies that demonstrate your value
- How to present data clearly in writing
- How to write executive summaries that people actually read

> The ability to write a clear, compelling business document is one of the most valuable skills in any professional setting. AI makes it achievable for anyone.

---

## Why Written Reports and Proposals Matter

Business decisions get made based on documents. A funding decision. A project approval. A client choosing you over a competitor. A manager deciding to move forward with your recommendation.

The quality of the document directly affects the quality of the decision — and whether the decision goes your way.

Most people write reports that are too long, too vague, or too technical for the audience. AI helps you write documents that are clear, structured, and purpose-built for the reader.

## Part 1: Business Reports

### Types of Business Reports

| Report Type | Purpose |
|-------------|---------|
| Status/Progress Report | Update stakeholders on a project in progress |
| Feasibility Report | Evaluate whether an idea or project is viable |
| Market Research Report | Document findings from market research |
| Financial Report | Summarize financial performance |
| Incident/Problem Report | Document an issue that occurred and how it was resolved |
| Recommendation Report | Present analysis and recommend a course of action |

### The Universal Report Structure

Most business reports follow this structure:

1. **Title Page** — Report title, author, date, intended audience
2. **Executive Summary** — The entire report in 1 paragraph (written last)
3. **Table of Contents** — For reports longer than 5 pages
4. **Introduction** — Context and purpose
5. **Background/Findings** — The main body of information
6. **Analysis** — What the data/findings mean
7. **Recommendations** — What should happen next
8. **Conclusion** — Brief wrap-up
9. **Appendix** — Supporting data, charts, references

### Writing a Business Report with AI

> Prompt: "Write a business report on the following topic: [describe — e.g., 'the feasibility of opening a second location for a small coffee shop in Dubai']. The audience is [describe — e.g., the business owner and a potential investor]. Include: executive summary, introduction, background on the market, key findings, analysis of risks and opportunities, recommendations, and a conclusion. Tone: professional and data-focused."

> Prompt: "Here are the key data points and findings I have: [list your information]. Help me write the 'Findings' and 'Analysis' sections of a business report based on this information. Structure the findings clearly and draw meaningful conclusions in the analysis."

### Writing the Executive Summary

The executive summary is written last but read first. It should answer:
- What is this report about?
- What did we find?
- What should we do?

> Prompt: "Based on this report, write a 1-paragraph executive summary (150 words maximum). A busy executive who only reads this paragraph should understand the situation, the key findings, and the recommended action."

### Exercise 1
- [ ] Choose a business scenario (e.g., "Should my business start using social media advertising?").
- [ ] Draft a short feasibility or recommendation report (4–6 sections) using AI.
- [ ] Write the executive summary last.
- [ ] Check: does your executive summary accurately capture everything in the report?

## Part 2: Project Proposals

### The Purpose of a Project Proposal

A project proposal is used to:
- Pitch a project idea to a decision-maker (internal or external)
- Win a contract from a client
- Secure budget or resources for an initiative

It is different from a business plan (which covers an entire business) — a proposal is scoped to a specific project or engagement.

### Proposal vs. SOW

| | Proposal | Statement of Work |
|-|----------|-------------------|
| Purpose | Win the project | Define the project's terms |
| When | Before agreement | After agreement |
| Tone | Persuasive | Operational |
| Focus | Why you | How it works |

### Structure of a Strong Project Proposal

1. **Cover Page** — Project title, client name, your name, date
2. **Executive Summary** — Problem + solution in 2–3 sentences
3. **Problem Statement** — The client's challenge, described in their language
4. **Proposed Solution** — Your approach and why it's the right one
5. **Scope of Work** — What you will deliver, broken into clear phases or deliverables
6. **Timeline** — Project phases with estimated dates
7. **Investment** — Pricing, payment schedule
8. **Your Credentials** — Why you're qualified (brief, evidence-based)
9. **Next Steps** — What happens when they say yes

### Writing a Proposal with AI

> Prompt: "Write a professional project proposal for the following situation: I am a [your role] and a client needs help with [describe the project]. My proposed solution is [describe approach]. The key deliverables are [list them]. The timeline is [estimated duration]. My price is [amount or range]. Write a full proposal with all standard sections. Tone: confident, solution-focused, professional."

### The Problem Statement — Most Overlooked Section

Most proposals jump straight to "here's what I'll do." The strongest proposals start by demonstrating that you deeply understand the client's problem.

> Prompt: "Write a problem statement for this proposal that shows I understand the client's challenge. The situation is: [describe]. Make the client feel seen and understood — not lectured at. Keep it under 150 words."

### Pricing Presentation Tips

How you present price matters as much as the number itself. Use AI to structure it well:

> Prompt: "Write a pricing section for a proposal where the total is [amount]. Break it down by phase or deliverable so it doesn't feel like a single large number. Include a note about the payment schedule (e.g., 50% on signing, 50% on delivery). Frame the investment in terms of the value it provides."

### Exercise 2
- [ ] Write a full project proposal for a service you could offer (web design, social media management, business consulting, photography, etc.).
- [ ] Focus especially on the Problem Statement and the Solution sections.
- [ ] Ask AI to play the role of the client and critique the proposal: "What questions or objections would you have after reading this proposal if you were the client?"
- [ ] Address at least 2 of the objections in the proposal itself.

## Part 3: Case Studies

### What Is a Business Case Study?

A case study is a document that tells the story of how you helped a client achieve a result. It's one of the most powerful marketing tools you can have because it shows proof, not just promises.

### The Case Study Formula

The best case studies follow a simple narrative arc:

**Challenge → Approach → Result**

Every reader wants to know: "Was this person able to help someone like me?"

### Structure

1. **Client Overview** — Brief description of the client (industry, size, context)
2. **The Challenge** — What problem they were facing before you got involved
3. **Your Approach** — What you did and why (not a full technical breakdown — tell the story)
4. **The Results** — Specific, measurable outcomes (numbers are gold)
5. **Client Quote** — A testimonial if you have one
6. **Takeaway / Call to Action** — What a reader should do next

### Writing a Case Study with AI

> Prompt: "Write a business case study based on the following information: Client: [describe]. Problem they had: [describe]. What I did: [describe]. Results achieved: [describe — be specific with numbers if possible]. Format it as a compelling 1-page case study with the structure: Client Overview, Challenge, Approach, Results, and a closing takeaway."

If you don't have a real client yet, write a hypothetical one:

> Prompt: "Write a hypothetical case study for a [type of service provider] who helped a [type of client] achieve [result]. Make it realistic and specific — use plausible numbers and a realistic industry context. This will be used as an example case study template."

### Making Results Specific

Vague: "We improved their social media presence."
Strong: "Follower count grew from 800 to 4,200 in 90 days. Engagement rate increased by 340%. Two new clients came directly from Instagram."

> Prompt: "The results section of my case study is too vague. Here's what actually happened: [describe]. Help me express these results in specific, quantified, compelling language."

### Exercise 3
- [ ] Write a case study for one project you've worked on (professional or personal).
- [ ] If you have no professional experience yet, write a case study for helping a friend, family member, or school project.
- [ ] Make sure at least one result is expressed with a specific number or percentage.

## Part 4: Data Presentation in Written Documents

### The Problem with Data in Reports

Most people either:
1. Dump raw data without explaining what it means
2. Describe data in long paragraphs when a table or chart would be clearer

### Using AI to Interpret and Present Data

> Prompt: "Here is some data I need to include in a business report: [paste data or describe what you have — e.g., monthly sales figures, survey results]. Help me: (1) interpret what this data is telling us, (2) summarize the key insight in one sentence, and (3) write a 2-paragraph 'analysis' section for the report that explains the findings to a non-technical reader."

### Turning Numbers into Narratives

The best business writers translate numbers into meaning:

Instead of: "Revenue in Q3 was AED 450,000, compared to AED 380,000 in Q2."
Write: "Revenue grew 18% quarter-over-quarter in Q3, driven primarily by increased client retention and two large new contracts signed in August."

> Prompt: "Here are some numbers from my business: [list the key figures]. Help me write a paragraph that tells the story behind these numbers — what they mean for the business, what's going well, and where attention is needed."

### Recommending Tables vs. Charts

> Prompt: "I have this data: [describe]. Should I present this as a table, a bar chart, a line graph, or in plain text? Explain why, and then write the descriptive text that would accompany whichever format is best."

### Exercise 4

- [ ] Ask AI to write an analysis section for a business report based on those numbers.
- [ ] Identify one place where a table would present the data more clearly than prose.

## Part 5: Writing for Different Audiences

### The Single Most Common Report Writing Mistake

Writing for yourself instead of for your reader.

A financial report written for a bank manager reads differently from the same information presented to an operations team. A progress report for your boss reads differently from one for a client.

### Adapting Your Writing with AI

> Prompt: "Here is a section from a report I've written: [paste]. The original audience was [describe]. I now need to present the same information to [describe new audience — e.g., a non-technical business owner, a technical team, a teenager, an investor]. Rewrite it for the new audience — adjust the language, level of detail, and emphasis."

### Levels of Formality

| Audience | Tone | Vocabulary |
|----------|------|-----------|
| C-suite executive | Formal, direct | Business language, minimal jargon |
| Technical team | Professional | Technical terminology acceptable |
| General public | Plain language | No jargon, examples and analogies |
| Investor | Persuasive, confident | Business and financial language |
