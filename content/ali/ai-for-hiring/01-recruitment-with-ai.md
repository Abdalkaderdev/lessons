# Recruitment & Hiring with AI

## What You'll Learn

- How to write compelling, inclusive job descriptions using AI
- How to build screening criteria that filter the right candidates
- How to generate role-specific interview questions in minutes

> This lesson is practical. By the end, you will have a complete hiring toolkit — job description, screening rubric, interview questions, and offer letter template — ready to use for a real role.

---

## Why Hiring Is One of the Most Important Business Skills

The people you hire shape everything: your culture, your output, your growth rate, and your reputation. A bad hire costs far more than a missed hire — in time, money, and morale.

Most founders and managers treat hiring as an afterthought. They write vague job posts, run unstructured interviews, and make decisions based on gut feel. The result is inconsistent teams and high turnover.

AI won't make the hiring decision for you. But it will help you:
- Write better job descriptions that attract the right people
- Ask better questions that reveal real capability
- Evaluate candidates more fairly and consistently
- Move faster without sacrificing quality

## Part 1: Writing Job Descriptions with AI

### Why Most Job Descriptions Fail

A poor job description either attracts nobody or attracts everyone — neither is useful. Common problems:
- Too vague ("must be a team player, self-starter")
- Too long (no one reads 800-word job posts)
- Focused on requirements rather than the opportunity
- Missing key information (compensation, location, growth path)

### The Anatomy of a Strong Job Description

A great job post includes:
1. **Role title** — specific and searchable
2. **What the company does** — one paragraph, plain English
3. **What this person will actually do** — 4–6 bullet points of real tasks
4. **What success looks like** — measurable outcomes at 30, 60, 90 days
5. **Requirements** — split into "must have" vs. "nice to have"
6. **What you offer** — salary range, benefits, flexibility, growth
7. **How to apply** — clear next steps

### Writing a Job Description with Claude

Go to [claude.ai](https://claude.ai) and use this prompt:

> Prompt: "Write a job description for a [job title] at [company type]. The company [brief description]. This role will be responsible for [main responsibilities]. We're looking for someone with [key skills/experience]. We offer [salary/benefits]. Format it with: Company Overview, What You'll Do (5 bullet points), What Success Looks Like at 90 Days, Requirements (split into Must Have and Nice to Have), and What We Offer. Keep the tone [professional/casual/startup-friendly]."

**Example:**

> Prompt: "Write a job description for a Social Media Manager at a small e-commerce fashion brand in Dubai. The company sells modest fashion online to women aged 18–35 across the GCC. This role will manage Instagram, TikTok, and Pinterest, create content calendars, work with influencers, and report on performance. We need someone with 1–3 years of experience and knowledge of Arabic and English content. We offer AED 8,000–12,000/month plus remote Fridays. Format with: Company Overview, What You'll Do, 90-Day Success Metrics, Requirements (Must Have vs. Nice to Have), and What We Offer."

### LinkedIn Recruiter Tips

When posting on [LinkedIn](https://linkedin.com):
- Use LinkedIn's built-in "Skills" section — it helps their algorithm surface your post to the right candidates
- Set a specific location or "Remote" — vague location settings hurt reach
- Ask a specific screening question in the application (e.g., "Describe a campaign you ran and the result")
- LinkedIn's free tier lets you post jobs but limits applicant management — use it as a discovery channel, then move to email

## Part 2: Building Screening Criteria

### The Problem with Unstructured Screening

When you get 50 applications and start reading them randomly, you'll unconsciously favor candidates who seem most like you or who apply first. This leads to biased, inconsistent decisions.

Screening criteria are your filter before you spend time on interviews. They define the minimum bar a candidate must clear to move forward.

### Types of Screening Criteria

**Knock-out criteria** — disqualifying factors:
- Must be based in [city] or willing to relocate
- Must have experience with [specific tool or skill]
- Must be available to start within [timeframe]

**Ranking criteria** — factors that make one candidate stronger than another:
- Years of relevant experience
- Demonstrated results (not just responsibilities)
- Quality of portfolio or work samples
- Clarity and professionalism of application

### Creating a Screening Rubric with AI

> Prompt: "I'm hiring a [job title] for a [type of company]. Help me create a structured screening rubric to evaluate applications. I need: (1) 3 knock-out criteria that immediately disqualify a candidate, (2) 5 ranking criteria with a 1–5 scoring system, and (3) 2 screening questions to send to promising applicants before a phone call. The role involves [main responsibilities]."

Use [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com) or [Claude](https://claude.ai) — both work well for this.

## Part 3: Interview Question Generation

### Why Most Interview Questions Are Useless

"What's your greatest weakness?" tells you nothing. Neither does "Where do you see yourself in five years?" These questions get rehearsed answers, not real insight.

Strong interview questions are:
- **Behavioral** — based on what the candidate has actually done
- **Situational** — how they'd handle a real scenario from your business
- **Role-specific** — testing the actual skills they'll use in the job

### The STAR Framework

Great behavioral answers follow the STAR format:
- **S**ituation — what was the context?
- **T**ask — what were they responsible for?
- **A**ction — what did they specifically do?
- **R**esult — what was the measurable outcome?

Train candidates to answer this way, and train yourself to listen for it.

### Generating Interview Questions by Role

> Prompt: "I'm interviewing candidates for a [job title] role. Generate 10 interview questions: 4 behavioral questions (starting with 'Tell me about a time...'), 3 situational questions (starting with 'Imagine you...' or 'What would you do if...'), 2 role-specific skill questions, and 1 culture/values question. The role involves [main responsibilities]. We are a [type of company] that values [key values]."

For senior roles, add:

> Prompt: "Add 3 questions to assess leadership and decision-making for a senior [role] at a growing startup."

## Part 4: Candidate Evaluation Frameworks

### Comparing Candidates Fairly

After interviewing three or more candidates, your memory starts blending them together. A structured evaluation framework prevents this and protects you from making purely emotional decisions.

### The Scorecard Method

Rate every candidate on the same dimensions:

| Dimension | Weight | Candidate A | Candidate B | Candidate C |
|-----------|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Relevant experience | 20% | 4 | 3 | 5 |
| Communication skills | 20% | 5 | 4 | 3 |
| Problem-solving | 25% | 4 | 5 | 4 |
| Culture fit | 15% | 5 | 3 | 4 |
| Growth potential | 20% | 3 | 5 | 4 |
| **Weighted Total** | | 4.1 | 4.0 | 4.0 |

### Building a Custom Scorecard with AI

> Prompt: "Create a candidate evaluation scorecard for a [job title] role. Include 5–7 evaluation dimensions relevant to this role, assign percentage weights to each (totalling 100%), and provide a brief guide on what a score of 1, 3, and 5 looks like for each dimension. Make it objective enough that two different interviewers would score similarly."

## Part 5: Drafting Offer Letters with AI

### What an Offer Letter Contains

An offer letter is a formal document that outlines:
- Job title and start date
- Compensation (salary, bonus, equity if applicable)
- Benefits
- Reporting structure
- Employment type (full-time, contract, probationary period)
- Conditions (subject to background check, references, etc.)
- Expiry date for the offer

> Important: Offer letters are legal documents. Always have a lawyer or HR professional review offer letters before sending them, especially if they include equity, non-compete clauses, or IP assignment. AI can draft — a human should review.

### Drafting an Offer Letter with AI

> Prompt: "Draft a professional offer letter for a [job title] position. Details: Start date [date], Salary [amount and currency], Working hours [e.g., Sunday–Thursday, 9am–6pm], Location [city/remote], Probation period [e.g., 3 months], Reporting to [role]. Include a section for the candidate to sign and date. Keep the tone [formal/warm but professional]. Add a standard clause about confidentiality and IP ownership."

Use [Claude](https://claude.ai) for this — it produces clean, structured document drafts.

### Customizing for Different Scenarios

- **Contract/freelance:** Swap salary for a day rate or project fee, add deliverables and payment terms
- **Internship:** Include learning objectives, mentorship commitment, and stipend
- **Senior hire with equity:** Add a placeholder equity section noting that vesting schedule details are in a separate agreement

## Putting It All Together

A full hiring workflow with AI looks like this:

1. **Write job description** → Claude or ChatGPT
2. **Post on LinkedIn** → with 2 screening questions
3. **Screen applications** → using your knock-out criteria first, then your rubric
4. **Interview shortlist** → using your 10-question bank, note STAR answers
5. **Score candidates** → using your evaluation scorecard
6. **Make the decision** → compare weighted scores, discuss with any co-founders or team
7. **Draft offer letter** → Claude, then reviewed by a professional

You've just built a hiring process that most companies with 50 employees don't have.
