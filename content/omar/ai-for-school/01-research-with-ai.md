# Research with AI

## What You'll Learn

- How to use Claude and ChatGPT to research topics for school projects
- How to write prompts that actually get you useful answers
- How to fact-check what AI tells you (this is crucial)
- How to compare AI-generated info with real sources
- How to use AI as a research partner, not a copy-paste machine

---

## Why This Matters

You've probably already used ChatGPT to look something up. Maybe you asked it a question and copy-pasted the answer. Here's the thing — that approach gets you a C at best and plagiarism trouble at worst.

But when you use AI *smartly*, it becomes the best research assistant you've ever had. It's like having a tutor who's read every book, available 24/7, and will explain anything at whatever level you need.

The key word is *partner*. AI helps you find and understand information. You still need to verify it, shape it, and make it yours.

---

## How AI Research Actually Works

When you ask ChatGPT or Claude a question, here's what's happening behind the scenes:

- The AI was trained on a massive amount of text from the internet, books, and articles
- It generates a response based on patterns in that training data
- It does NOT browse the internet in real-time (unless it tells you it has web search on)
- It can be confidently wrong — this is called a "hallucination"

That last point is everything. AI can sound totally sure of itself while being completely wrong. Dates, statistics, names, quotes — all of these can be fabricated.

**This means you always verify. Always.**

---

## Bad Prompts vs. Good Prompts

Most people ask AI questions like they're Googling. That gives you Google-level answers — surface stuff, generic, not that useful.

Here's how to level up:

### Bad prompt:
> "Tell me about climate change"

You'll get a 5-paragraph Wikipedia-style overview that tells you nothing new.

### Better prompt:
> Prompt: "I'm a 17-year-old writing a school essay on the economic impact of climate change on coastal cities. Give me 5 specific angles I could explore, with one key fact or statistic for each. Use simple language."

Now you're giving the AI:
- Your context (who you are, what the assignment is)
- A specific task (5 angles, key facts)
- A format request (specific, not general)
- A language level (simple)

---

## The Research Prompt Formula

Use this structure whenever you're starting research:

> Prompt: "I'm researching [TOPIC] for [PURPOSE]. I'm looking for [SPECIFIC THING — angles, facts, arguments, examples]. Please give me [FORMAT — a list, a summary, a breakdown]. Keep it [LEVEL — simple, detailed, technical]."

### Real examples:

> Prompt: "I'm researching the French Revolution for a history class essay. I need 3 strong arguments for why economic inequality was the main cause. Give me each argument in 2-3 sentences with a supporting fact."

> Prompt: "I'm doing a science project on black holes. Explain how they form in plain language a 17-year-old with no physics background would understand. Use an analogy if possible."

> Prompt: "Give me 5 perspectives on social media and teen mental health — both supporting and opposing the idea that it's harmful. I want to understand both sides before picking my essay stance."

---

## Digging Deeper: Follow-Up Prompts

One prompt is rarely enough. The real power is in the conversation.

After your first response, try:

- "Can you go deeper on point number 3?"
- "Give me a specific real-world example of that."
- "I don't understand the part about [X]. Explain it differently."
- "What would be the strongest counter-argument to this?"
- "What are the most credible sources I could check to verify this?"

> Prompt: "You mentioned that 'rising sea levels threaten 1 billion people by 2050.' Where does that figure come from? Which organizations or studies should I look at to verify it?"

This last move is gold — ask AI to point you to the real sources instead of trusting the AI-generated fact itself.

## How to Fact-Check AI Research

This is the most important skill in this whole lesson. Here's your fact-checking checklist:

**Step 1: Flag anything specific**
Numbers, dates, names, quotes, statistics — these are the things most likely to be wrong. Underline them mentally.

**Step 2: Ask AI for its source**
> Prompt: "What's the source for that statistic? Give me the author, publication, and year if possible."

The AI might not know — but it might point you in the right direction.

**Step 3: Cross-reference with real sources**
- Google Scholar (scholar.google.com) for academic papers
- BBC, Reuters, AP News for current events
- Government or official organization websites (.gov, .org)
- Wikipedia is fine as a starting point — always check its sources at the bottom

**Step 4: The "three source rule"**
If you can't find at least two independent sources confirming something, don't use it as a fact in your work.

**Step 5: Check the date**
AI training data has a cutoff. If you're researching something that changed in the last year or two, the AI might be outdated.

## Comparing Sources: AI vs. Real Sources

Think of it like a research triangle:

```
        AI (starting point)
       /                    \
Google Scholar          News/Official Sites
       \                    /
         Your Essay
```

- Use AI to understand the landscape and generate questions
- Use real sources to verify facts and find quotes you can actually cite
- Never cite AI itself as a source in a school essay

## Platform Differences: Claude vs. ChatGPT

Both are great, but they have different strengths:

| | ChatGPT | Claude |
|---|---|---|
| Web search | Yes (with GPT-4o) | Yes (with Claude.ai) |
| Long documents | OK | Better for long stuff |
| Tone | Slightly more casual | Slightly more precise |
| Best for | Quick research, brainstorming | Longer analysis, nuanced topics |

Try both on the same question and compare the answers. Different perspectives from different AIs can actually help you see a topic more fully.

## Real Research Workflow

Here's how to actually use this in a school project:

**1. Start broad**
> Prompt: "Give me an overview of [TOPIC]. What are the 5 most important things to understand about it?"

**2. Find your angle**
> Prompt: "Here are 3 possible essay angles I'm considering: [list them]. Which one is most interesting and arguable? What's the strongest evidence for each?"

**3. Go deep on your angle**
> Prompt: "I've chosen to argue [THESIS]. Give me 4 supporting points with evidence. Also give me 2 counter-arguments I'll need to address."

**4. Get sources to check**
> Prompt: "What should I search on Google Scholar or news sites to find real sources for these points?"

**5. Verify everything**
Go check those sources. Adjust your essay based on what you actually find.

## Exercises

- [ ] Pick any topic you're studying in school right now. Write a research prompt using the formula from this lesson. Compare the response to what you'd find from a quick Google search. Note: what did AI give you that Google didn't? What was missing?

- [ ] Take any three facts from an AI response. Fact-check each one using at least two outside sources. Write down whether each fact was: accurate, partially accurate, or wrong.

- [ ] Ask AI the same question on Claude AND ChatGPT. Compare the answers. Write 3 differences you notice.

- [ ] Practice a multi-turn research session: start with a broad question, then ask 3 follow-up prompts to go deeper. Write down how the quality of information changed with each follow-up.

- [ ] Research a topic you know well (a hobby, a sport, something you're into). Ask AI about it and spot at least one thing it gets wrong or oversimplifies.

## Quick Reference: Research Prompt Templates

Save these and use them:

> Prompt: "Give me an overview of [TOPIC] for someone who knows nothing about it. Then give me 5 angles I could write about for a school essay."

> Prompt: "I want to argue [POSITION]. Give me the 3 strongest arguments for this, each with one supporting fact or example. Also give me the main counter-argument."

> Prompt: "Explain [CONCEPT] using an analogy. Then explain it again more technically. I want to understand it at both levels."

> Prompt: "What would a [historian / scientist / economist / etc.] say about [TOPIC]? Give me their perspective specifically."

> Prompt: "What's still debated or uncertain about [TOPIC]? Where do experts disagree?"
