## Why People Fail Interviews

Interviews are rarely failed because the candidate lacked skills.
They are failed because of:
- Not preparing answers in advance
- Not knowing anything about the company
- Rambling answers with no structure
- Nervous energy that reads as lack of confidence

AI fixes all of these. You prepare the answers, you research the company,
you practice until the answers sound natural — and then the interview is easy.

## The Most Common Interview Questions (And Why They Are Asked)

| Question | What they are really asking |
|----------|-----------------------------|
| Tell me about yourself | Can you communicate clearly? Are you self-aware? |
| Why do you want this job? | Did you research us? Are you genuinely interested? |
| What are your strengths? | Do you know what you are good at? |
| What is your biggest weakness? | Are you honest and self-aware? |
| Tell me about a time you solved a problem | Can you handle challenges? Do you take initiative? |
| Where do you see yourself in 5 years? | Are you ambitious? Will you stay? |
| Do you have any questions for us? | Are you serious about this role? |

Every question is testing something. When you know what it is testing, you can answer it properly.

## The STAR Method

For behavioural questions ("Tell me about a time..."), always use STAR:

- **S**ituation — What was the context?
- **T**ask — What needed to be done?
- **A**ction — What did YOU specifically do?
- **R**esult — What happened because of your actions?

Keep each part short. Total answer: 60–90 seconds.

**Example using school project as experience:**

"At school I was part of a group project for our IT class. (Situation)
We needed to present a working website in two weeks but two members of the group
were not contributing. (Task) I spoke to them individually, found out one was struggling
with the design work, so I offered to handle design while they focused on the research.
(Action) We submitted on time, got a good grade, and our teacher highlighted our
project as one of the best. (Result)"

No paid work experience needed. School, personal projects, and everyday life are full of STAR stories.

## Step 2: Prepare Your "Tell Me About Yourself" Answer

This is the most important question because it is almost always asked first.

It should last 60–90 seconds and cover:
1. Who you are (briefly)
2. Relevant experience or projects
3. Why you are interested in this specific role

> Prompt: "Write a 'Tell me about yourself' answer for a job interview. I'm Omar, 17, studying at [school]. I have built web projects using AI tools including a portfolio page, a quiz app, and a calculator app. I'm interested in technology and creative work. I am applying for [job title] at [company]. The answer should be 60–90 seconds when read aloud. Confident but not arrogant. Specific but not overloaded with details."

Practice saying it aloud. Time yourself. Adjust until it flows naturally.

## Step 3: Prepare Your STAR Stories

Think of 3–5 situations from your life you can use to answer "tell me about a time" questions.
They do not need to be work situations.

Good sources:
- School group project that went well (or badly — and you helped fix it)
- Time you learned something difficult quickly
- Time you helped someone solve a problem
- Time you organised something (event, trip, project)
- Time you disagreed with someone and handled it maturely

For each story, ask Claude to help you structure it:

> Prompt: "I want to use this as an interview answer: [describe your situation in plain words]. Help me structure it using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result). Make it 60–90 seconds when spoken. Make the 'Action' part emphasise what I specifically did, not what the team did."

Write your final STAR stories in a notes file. Read them several times until they feel natural.

## Step 4: Research the Company With AI

"Why do you want to work here?" is almost always asked. If you cannot answer it
with specifics, you look like you applied to 50 jobs and do not actually care about this one.

AI makes company research fast.

> Prompt: "Tell me about [company name]. What do they sell or do? Who are their customers? What is the company culture like? What recent news or developments should I know about as a job applicant? What makes them different from competitors? Give me the key facts I need to answer 'Why do you want to work here?' in an interview."

Then prepare your answer:

> Prompt: "Based on that company overview, help me write a 'Why do you want to work here?' answer. I'm genuinely interested in [aspect of the company]. I am 17 and this would be my first job. Keep the answer to 45–60 seconds. Specific — not 'because you're a great company.'"

---

## Step 5: Prepare Your Weakness Answer

The weakness question trips people up. Two bad approaches:
1. Fake weakness disguised as a strength: "I just work too hard" (interviewers hate this)
2. Genuine weakness with no follow-up: "I get nervous presenting" (ends conversation)

The good approach: Real weakness + what you are doing about it.

> Prompt: "Help me answer 'What is your biggest weakness?' for a job interview. I'm 17 with no work experience. My genuine weakness is [describe a real one — e.g. 'I sometimes take too long to start tasks because I overthink them']. Write an answer that is honest, shows self-awareness, and includes what I am actively doing to improve. Keep it to 30–45 seconds. Do not make it sound scripted or fake."

---

## Step 6: Questions to Ask the Interviewer

At the end of an interview, they ask: "Do you have any questions for us?"

Saying "No" is a mistake. It signals lack of interest.

Good questions to ask:
- "What does a typical day look like in this role?"
- "What would make someone really successful in this position?"
- "How does the team here support new starters?"
- "What do you enjoy most about working here?"
- "What are the next steps in the hiring process?"

Avoid:
- Anything about salary (first interview is too early)
- Questions whose answers are on the company website

> Prompt: "I'm interviewing at [company] for [role]. Write 5 smart questions I can ask at the end of the interview. They should show genuine interest in the company and role. I'm 17 applying for my first job. Make them sound natural, not corporate."

---

## Step 7: The Thank-You Email

After every interview, send a thank-you email within 24 hours. Most candidates do not do this.
It makes you memorable and professional.

> Prompt: "Write a thank-you email I can send after a job interview at [company] for [role]. The interview was with [interviewer name or just 'the hiring manager']. It should: thank them for their time, mention one specific thing we discussed in the interview (I'll add this detail), restate my enthusiasm for the role, and end with looking forward to next steps. Keep it short — 5–7 sentences. Professional but not stiff. Do not start with 'I hope this email finds you well.'"

Send it the same evening or first thing the next morning.

## Full Mock Interview Exercise

Put everything together. This exercise takes 20–30 minutes.

### Setup

Go to [claude.ai](https://claude.ai) and paste this:

> Prompt: "Conduct a full mock interview with me. I'm 17, applying for [job title] at [company]. Ask me these questions, one at a time, and wait for my real answer before proceeding: (1) Tell me about yourself, (2) Why do you want to work here?, (3) What are your strengths?, (4) What is your biggest weakness?, (5) Tell me about a time you had to solve a problem or overcome a challenge, (6) Where do you see yourself in 3 years?, (7) Do you have any questions for us? After each of my answers, give detailed feedback: what worked, what was weak, and a specific suggestion to improve it. Begin."

### Rules

- Answer each question as if it is a real interview (do not look at notes)
- Give yourself 30 seconds to think before each answer
- Do not skip questions — answer all 7

After Claude gives feedback on all 7, ask:

> Prompt: "Based on all my answers today, what are my top 3 weaknesses as an interview candidate? What should I practice most before a real interview?"

Do this exercise twice this week with different companies and roles.

## Online Interviews

Many first-round interviews are now video calls (Teams, Zoom, Google Meet).

Tips:
- Test your camera and microphone the night before
- Find a quiet room with a neutral background
- Dress professionally — at least from the waist up
- Look at the camera, not the screen, when you answer (makes eye contact)
- Have your notes visible but off-screen

Ask Claude to prep you specifically:

> Prompt: "I have a video interview on [platform] for [role]. What are the specific things I should prepare for an online interview that are different from in-person? Give me a checklist for the day before and the hour before."
