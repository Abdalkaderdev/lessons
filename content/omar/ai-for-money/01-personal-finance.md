## Why Money Knowledge at 17 Is a Superpower

Compound interest is the most powerful force in personal finance.

If you save £100/month from age 17 and invest it at a 7% average annual return:
- By age 30: ~£28,000
- By age 40: ~£87,000
- By age 50: ~£228,000

If you start the same habit at age 27:
- By age 50: ~£114,000

Starting at 17 instead of 27 means you end up with **twice as much money** by 50,
doing the exact same thing. That is the power of starting early.

You do not need a lot of money to start. You need a habit and a plan.

## Saving vs Spending — The Mindset Shift

Most people think budgeting means "not spending money on fun things."
That is wrong. Budgeting means knowing where your money goes so you can decide
what it does for you.

**The three types of spending:**

1. **Needs** — things you must pay (food, transport, phone)
2. **Wants** — things you choose to pay (clothes, takeaways, entertainment)
3. **Future self** — money you set aside to grow (savings, investment)

The goal is not to eliminate wants. It is to make sure your future self gets a share
before your present self spends everything.

A simple starting target:
- 50% Needs
- 30% Wants
- 20% Future self (savings)

At 17, you might not have many needs to pay yet. That makes it even easier to
build the savings habit now while your expenses are low.

## Step 1: Brain Dump Your Current Money

Before budgeting, you need to know your actual numbers.

Go to [claude.ai](https://claude.ai) and answer these questions (copy and fill in your numbers):

> Prompt: "Help me create a monthly budget. Here is my current financial situation: Monthly income: [e.g. £200 from part-time work + £50 pocket money = £250]. Regular expenses I know about: [e.g. phone bill £15, bus pass £40, lunch money £80, subscriptions £10]. I usually spend money on: [e.g. clothes, food with friends, games]. I currently save: [e.g. £0 or whatever you save]. I'm 17 in the UK. Create a clear monthly budget for me that includes a savings target. Show it as a simple table."

Claude will produce a budget table. Review it — does it match reality?

## Step 2: Build Your Monthly Budget

Once you have the raw numbers, ask Claude to build a proper budget:

> Prompt: "Based on my financial situation above, create a monthly budget using the 50/30/20 rule adapted for a 17-year-old (50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings). Show me: (1) what my ideal allocation is for each category, (2) where I'm currently over or under spending, (3) one specific thing I could cut to hit my savings target. Present it clearly in a table format."

Print this out or save it somewhere you see it weekly.

## Step 3: Understand Banking Basics

You need to understand how banking works before you can use it well.

Ask Claude to explain:

> Prompt: "Explain UK banking to me like I'm 17 and have never had a bank account. Cover: what a current account is, what a savings account is, what interest rates mean (both on savings and on debt), what an overdraft is and why it can be dangerous, what a debit card vs credit card is. Use simple language and real examples with small amounts of money."

Key things to know:

**Current Account** — your day-to-day account. Money goes in, money goes out.
Debit card linked to it. Best options for under-18s:
- [Monzo](https://monzo.com) (16+)
- [Starling](https://starlingbank.com) (16+)
- [HSBC Student Account](https://hsbc.co.uk)

**Savings Account** — a separate account where money earns interest.
Even a small interest rate (2–5%) is better than keeping money in a current account.

**Interest Rate** — the percentage your savings grow per year.
Example: £500 in a savings account with 4% interest = £520 after one year.

**Overdraft** — spending more than you have. The bank covers it — but charges you.
Avoid overdrafts. They are expensive and habit-forming.

## Step 4: Credit Cards and Credit Scores

You cannot get a credit card at 17 in the UK. But you can start building your
credit score by understanding how it works.

> Prompt: "Explain credit scores to me like I'm 17. What is a credit score, how is it calculated, why does it matter, and what can I do right now at 17 to start building a good credit history so I'm in a strong position when I'm 18? Use plain English and UK-specific advice."

Key facts:
- Credit score = how trustworthy you are with borrowed money
- A high score = better interest rates on loans, easier to rent a flat, sometimes needed for jobs
- You start building it at 18 when you can get your first credit card
- The habit that matters most: pay your bills on time, every time

## Step 5: Investing Basics

Investing is not just for adults with spare cash. Starting small at 17 builds the habit.

> Prompt: "Explain the basics of investing to me like I'm 17. I have no investment experience. Cover: what investing is, why it beats keeping money in cash long-term, the difference between stocks, funds, and index funds, what risk means, and what someone my age should realistically consider starting with. Use plain English and UK-specific examples."

Key concepts explained simply:

**Stock** — buying a tiny piece of a company. If the company grows, your share is worth more.

**Index Fund** — instead of one company, you own a tiny piece of hundreds of companies.
Less risky because if one company fails, the others carry the fund.

**Compound returns** — your returns earn returns. The longer you invest, the faster it grows.
This is why starting at 17 is so powerful.

**Platforms you can explore at 18:**
- [Vanguard](https://vanguard.co.uk) — low-cost index funds
- [Freetrade](https://freetrade.io) — buy stocks from your phone, no commission
- [Trading 212](https://trading212.com) — invest in fractional shares (you can start with £1)

At 17 you can ask a parent to set up a Junior ISA (JISA) in your name:
> A **Junior ISA** lets you save up to £9,000/year tax-free until you turn 18, when it converts to an adult ISA.

## The Emergency Fund Rule

Before investing a single pound, build an emergency fund.

An emergency fund is 1–3 months of expenses saved in a savings account, never touched
except for a genuine emergency (phone breaks, urgent travel, family situation).

For most 17-year-olds, that might be £200–£500.

Ask Claude:

> Prompt: "I'm 17 and want to build a small emergency fund. My monthly expenses are about [£X]. How much should my emergency fund be? How long will it take me to save it if I put [£Y] aside each month? Give me a simple savings plan."
